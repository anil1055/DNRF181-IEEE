**Background**
Software effort estimation (SEE) is essential for effective software project management, and it plays an important
role in planning, budgeting, and resource allocation [1] . An accurate estimate guarantees project completion on
schedule and on budget [2] ; anything other than an accurate estimate may result in lack of resources, missed
deadlines, or lost funds [3] . Although software dependability (SEE) is important, it is still difficult to achieve because
of the unavoidable complexity and variability associated with software development processes [4] . Traditional
methods such as Constructive Cost Model (COCOMO), Function Points Analysis (FPA), and Expert Judgment
often resort to trivial simple mathematical models or subjective evaluations that fail to consider the complexity

.
of the relationships among specific project characteristics and required effort [5]

Traditional Software Engineering Estimation (SEE) methods are constrained by multiple drawbacks, such
as the reliance on relative simplifications, where models such as COCOMO generalize or oversimplify linear
dependencies between input variables (e.g., size and complexity) and effort, which typically are not validated in
real-life contexts [6] . It is unchanging in nature and does not have the capacity to adapt or incorporate changing
dynamics in project environments [7] . subjectivity, in which expert judgment and similar estimation methods are
defined by human bias, which injects variability into results, coupled with a scalability problem, where these
methods cannot keep pace with increasingly larger and more complex data sets, and most importantly, they
cannot sum up a coherent analysis that considers the complexity of interaction among extreme events, all of
which are needed for studies in climate research [8] . Figure 1 shows the structure of the improving software effort
estimation.

In response to these challenges, recent research has turned to machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL)
techniques for SEE [9] . However, even state-of-the-art ML models face difficulties in hyperparameter tuning and

**Motivation for using deep learning and metaheuristic optimization**
Especially with regards to handling images from large datasets, deep learning networks, in particular,
convolutional neural networks (CNNs), tend to be successful in detecting complex patterns [11] . One of the variants
of CNN is the Inception Network, which has great merits for SEE given that it extracts hierarchical features and
is equipped to deal with high-dimensional space [12] . The performance of deep learning models centers greatly
on hyperparameter selection, which is often performed either manually or through grid/random search [13] . This
method, however, is typically computationally intensive, and prone to yield less-than-optimal approaches [14] . The
Inception-based method has the highest powers in software effort estimation (SEE) because it captures multiscale patterns in heterogeneous project attributes-such as size, team experience and contextual considerations-in
heterogeneous data at the same time through a number of parallel convolutional filters with different receptive

**Objectives**
In an effort to expand the boundaries with greater efficacy, Inception Network is integrated with an Enhanced
Banyan Tree Growth Optimizer (EBTGO), which is a metaheuristic growth dynamic based on the banyan tree
growth dynamic-albeit EBTGO is more adept in exploration and exploitation ways in order to perform efficient
and robust search hyperparameters optimization. The combination of these two very powerful techniques will
provide greater accuracy and trust in SEE.

Although the combination of deep learning (DL) with a metaheuristic optimizer, including Grey Wolf
Optimizer (GWO), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), or even Bayesian and grid-based search methods,
has been studied in numerous recent works, the proposed Inception-EBTGO model is a clear innovation in
architecture and optimization dynamics. In contrast to traditional methods of hyperparameter optimization,
which view hyperparameter optimization as a generic search problem, EBTGO is the only similar algorithm
that builds upon the adaptive growth processes of banyan trees-namely aerial root propagation, multi-trunk
development and spatial subgrouping-that allow a more balanced exploration/exploitation trade-off to be
achieved in high dimensional, sparse search spaces characteristic of software effort estimation.

Besides, whereas previous authors frequently use metaheuristics together with standard neural networks (e.g.,
MLPs or simple CNNs), our implementation of the Inception Network reconfigured to 1D tabular data permits

**Literature review**
Software effort estimation (SEE) has been long regarded as the second greatest challenge in software project
management, with the utmost attention of researchers constantly concentrated towards improving the accuracy
and dependability of the estimation models. Various methodologies have been proposed historically-from
classical techniques of parametric modeling formed on COCOMO to more advanced machine learning and
deep learning techniques [7] . The latest developments propose the use of integrating metaheuristic optimization
algorithms with existing ML and DL approaches in an attempt to alleviate and overcome the limitations of
conventional methods.

For instance, Khan et al. [17] made two contributions for estimation of software effort. Initially, the study
investigated the use of metaheuristic methods to generate a suitable parametric method for estimation of
software effort. Then, in order to demonstrate the advantages of using nature-inspired metaheuristic models
to optimize DL (Deep Learning) designs for estimation of software effort, the study provided a DNN (Deep
Neural Network) on the basis of metaheuristic methods. Actually, the SB (Strawberry) and GWO (Grey Wolf
Optimizer) methods were employed for used to providing a reasonable parametric method for estimation
of software effort. For validating the current methodsтАЩ enactment, a collection of 9 functions of benchmark
with large dimensions were used. The SB and GWOтАЩs results of the algorithms were contrasted with 5 different
metaheuristic techniques to estimate software effort. The findings demonstrated that the Grey Wolf Optimizer
had a clear advantage in estimating accuracy. The suggested Deep Neural Network method or GWDNNSB,
which employed metaheuristic techniques for selection of learning rate and primary weights, outperformed
previous study on employing Deep Neural Network for estimating software effort.

Sharma et al. [18] compared and assessed the effectiveness of 4 different techniques to estimation of effort:
AGANN (Adaptive GA-based neural network), LNI-NN (Localized Neighborhood Mutual Informationbased neural network), GEHO-based NFN, and NFL (Neuro-fuzzy logic). Each of the 4 modeling methods
were confirmed by means of 5 different datasets from the promise database: china, cocomo81, cocomonasa
v2, cocomonasa1, and desharnais. In fact, all of the effort estimate results were contrasted, also the output was
assessed by means of enactment evaluation measures like RMSE (root mean square error), mean magnitude of
relative error (MMRE), prediction accuracy (PRED), and median magnitude of relative error (MdMRE). The
findings revealed that GEHO-NFN (GEHO-based NFN) outperformed the other prominent soft computing
approaches.

Kassaymeh et al. [19] conducted the FCNN (fully connected neural network) method together with the GWO
or gray wolf optimizerтАЩs metaheuristic method known as GWO-FC, in order to address the issue of SEE (software

optimizer parameters through increasing the attained findingsтАЩ accuracy by means of enhancing the FCNNтАЩs
ability for exploring the parameter search field and preventing local optima. The suggested approach was

*Handling missing values*
Missing values do pose the risk of affecting the performance of models in a negative manner if treated badly or
not addressed. The following strategies have been applied for handling missing values according to the type of
feature:

Numerical Features: Missing values in numerical features were imputed using:
Mean Imputation: Making the mean of the feature the value that replaces missing values.
On the more sophisticated side, KNN provides a strong imputation method in that it predicts the unknown
values of missing data from the similarity of points from neighboring data points. KNN classification performs
better on a dataset when there are some feature dependencies with complexity regarding combinatorial
considerations.

Categorical features: Mode Imputation: Instead of estimating the mean, imputation would suggest the option
of replacing missing values with the most frequent category value.

Domain Knowledge Rules: Instead of taking the mode, we would take a reasonable default value defined by
domain knowledge. In relation to our use of the above strategies, we will have confidence in relation to increased
consistency and completeness of our data set and reduce the bias if any in the data that could have arisen through
missing occurrence of data.

The Mean Absolute Error (MAE) metrics are some of the most commonly used in assessing the accuracy of

**Ablation study**
We performed a rigorous ablation study to evaluate the effectiveness and necessity of our proposed EBTGO in
this framework by contrasting the performance of Inception Network under different optimization settings.
Specifically, we evaluated: (1) Inception Network without hyperparameter optimization (i.e., with default or
randomly chosen hyperparameters), (2) the same network optimized by Grid Search, (3) optimized by Bayesian
optimization, and (4) optimized by EBTGO as proposed. Therefore, this avenue would enable us to separate
the contribution of EBTGO and show its superiority over both types of non-optimization and regular tuning
methods.

All configurations were trained and validated on Maxwell and COCOMO81 datasets after exactly the same
preprocessing, model architecture, and evaluation metrics (MAE and MMRE). The Grid Search case performed

retains some diversity in the population through perturbing the best solution found so far and also re-sampling
for better solutions given threshold тАЬstagnationтАЭ and тАЬfitnessтАЭ levels so the search can move to be closer to a

that can become stuck at local optima due to an overreliance on the probabilistic model. These results show
conclusively that EBTGO is not simply an alternative to optimizing likelihood in other optimizers, but is a very
sine qua non for the models to perform at a high level.

The effectiveness of the EBTGO compared to the Bayesian Optimization and the Grid Search is not only due
to its efficiency in search but the biologically inspired algorithms that resonate with the ruggedness, sparsity and
non-convexity of hyperparameter landscapes in software effort estimation in deep learning. Contrary to Grid
Search, which blindly and exhaustively tries every combination without learning about past combinations, the
EBTGO intelligently modifies its searching path using aerial root propagation to explore all the promising areas
intensively and multi-trunk development in order to retain the exploratory diversity within subpopulations.

Conversely, Bayesian Optimization is based on a probabilistic surrogate model (e.g. Gaussian Process) that
assumes that the response surface is smooth and continuous- an assumption that is frequently not true in tabular
SEE data where small variations in hyperparameters can cause sudden changes in performance, since such data
is often sparse and exhibits noise.

Avoiding this trap with EBTGO is that it does not model the underlying response but works directly on fitness
feedback, which is more robust to discontinuities. In addition, its stagnation-aware perturbation policy (which
occurs when a candidate is not improving within t iterations) proactively avoids early convergence which is one
of the most frequent failure modes of Bayesian methods with small datasets such as Maxwell and COCOMO81.
Therefore, EBTGO can be strong in its structure-conscious search dynamics: it does not solve a generic blackbox problem, but instead an adaptive growth process that not only captures the complexity of the Inception
architecture, but also reflects the complexity of the underlying software project data, allowing the approach to be
used to find high-performing configurations with fewer, smarter evaluations.

By empowering the hybrid framework to efficiently search optimal hyperparameter configurations, with low
evaluation and time costs, it contributes uniquely, which adds to its opportunities to be deployed in any software
engineering environment constrained on either resources or time.

**Limitations and future work**
In the application of machine learning models, including the proposed Inception-EBTGO framework, for real-life
software project estimations, some broad-level issues arise surrounding fairness, transparency, interpretability,
and integration into systems. High accuracy is being observed for our model. However, these architectures,
especially deep learning ones, are often treated as black boxes, thereby making it doubly challenging for project
managers, stakeholders, or auditors to see how certain effort estimates were derived.

This lack of transparency lowers the trust and acceptance of the model in some collaborative and regulated
settings. Because this feature transparency is rather important, future work will integrate some post-hoc
explainability techniques, such as SHAP and LIME, to allow for insight into feature-level contributions toward
prediction outcomes, thus giving users a way to interpret the decisions of the model and identify possible sources
of bias.

There is also a risk of propagating or even exacerbating biases which already exist in historical datasetsinadequate or unfair representations of agile projects or the compositions of teams that leveled different
estimates against others-which would lead to the unfair estimation or skewed estimation of underrepresented
project types.

Thus, in the case of the SEE, addressing fairness issues becomes highly relevant especially when a budgetbuilding or staffing decision is being informed by the models. The more important considerations for smaller
teams or organizations that do not utilize any high-performance computing resources are that the overall costs
of training deep models and conducting meta-heuristic optimization could become prohibitive. We hope to
address this with lightweight alternatives to the models and perhaps with properly structured cloud tools for
scalable accessibility. Anything developed must also be fairly straightforward to integrate into existing project
management tools so that adoption is practical, meaning more focus on API-side integrations and real-time
estimates applicable for deployment in agile methodology. But collectively, it should be apparent at this stage
that it is supremely important to begin to replace accuracy simply with тАЬactualтАЭ humans interacting with the real
world as a superiorly holistic and industrially relevant AI estimating system.

While the encouraging results were achieved, this study has some limitations that need to be recognized for
better guidance for future research. Prominently, the model was assessed on two rather small benchmark datasetsMaxwell (62 projects) and COCOMO81 (81 projects)-which might have been widely used in SEE research but
do not do justice to the scale and complexity of modern software projects. Testing the scalability of the model
across larger, more heterogeneous, and higher-grade datasets (e.g., GitHub or proprietary repositories) is yet to
be seen. Second, the present implementation relies upon structured tabular project attributes.

Future extensions should study the fusion of unstructured data sources such as commit logs, issue trackers,
and developer communication for enhanced feature representation. Third, although EBTGO has good

optimization capabilities, its computational burden becomes heavier with an increase in hyperparameters and
data set sizes, obviously limiting its deployment claim in real-gal development environments.

Also, it assumes the static nature of project characteristics at the time of estimation, which is unlikely to hold

learning categories that allow for some incremental improvement of the model when confronted with new project
data. Finally, real-life challenges for deployment in this regard include the need for interpretability for project

and usability validation.

The use of two popular but comparatively small benchmark datasets and sets of roughly 62 projects (Maxwell)
and 81 projects (COCOMO81) is also a significant limitation of this study since, despite being standard in the
literature of software effort estimation (SEE), these datasets might not be representative of the size, variation, or
complexity of contemporary industrial software development.

We understand that the lack of such further datasets as NASA, Desharnais or ISBSG might lead to the
problem of generalizability. Nonetheless, this was due to methodological rigor: both Maxwell and COCOMO81
offer domain-consistent, well-documented, and pre-processed project attributes, with validated effort values,
and with minimal noise caused by inconsistent labeling or incomplete cost drivers, which are characteristic of
bigger repositories such as ISBSG.

In addition, these datasets are two different estimation paradigms: the empirical managerial qualities of
Maxwell as opposed to COCOMO81 and its organized cost-driving system provide a complementary context
of validation. With this said, we are consciously aware that this is a limitation on external validity, and future
efforts will involve assessing robustness on larger heterogeneous datasets, such as NASA93 and ISBSG, to larger
domains, project types, and scales.

**Conclusions**
Estimating software effort (SEE) is certainly rooted in a historical basis called project management. However,
conventional models like COCOMO and Function Points Analysis do not adequately explore the complexity of
the relationship between a project attribute and effort due to the simplicity of the models. Although machine
learning and deep learning appear to have solved some of these issues, they involve their own challenges,
mainly related to hyperparameter tuning and overfitting. The current research has attempted to fill this gap
by developing a truly innovative hybrid framework that incorporates the Inception Network and an Enhanced
Banyan Tree Growth Optimizer (EBTGO). The Inception Network is suitable for extracting high-dimensional
features in levels from data, while EBTGO allows for fine-tuning of critical hyperparameters to improve
generalization and accuracy in the model performance following the optimization process. Assessed using
the Maxwell and COCOMO81 datasets, the proposed model exceeded approximately six very strong recent
methods including COCOMO, Function Point Analysis (FPA), Random Forest (RF), Support Vector Regression
(SVR), Artificial Neural Network (ANN), and GEHO-NFN (GEHO-based Neuro-fuzzy Network) for important
metrics of interest, which include Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Magnitude of Relative Error (MMRE).
In addition, the evidence of superiority derives from different statistical tests and visual analyses, including ROC
curves and confusion matrices. This work demonstrates that mixed works have the potential to effectively address
the limitations of existing methods, thus showing that the hybrid nature (deep learning architectures fused with
metaheuristic optimization) can be revolutionary. These results suggest not only a major improvement in SEE,
but also allow opportunities for more novel innovation for predictive modeling in software engineering and
project management.
