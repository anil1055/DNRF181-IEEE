**I.** **Introduction**

Accurate and reliable effort estimate of the software

project under development is the most crucial
information for the project managers. Having this vital
information allows them to make more effective project
planning, control and staffing, therefore leading to
project delivered on time and within budget. Whereas,


managing the software project without accurate estimate
makes the task of the project manager more difficult and
stressful. As a result, he/she may take wrong decisions
threatening the success of the whole project. As case in
point, when the software project is under-estimated, the
manager probably will under-staff it (resulting in staff
burnout), under-scope the quality assurance effort
(running the risk of delivering poor quality product), and
set too short schedule (resulting in loss of credibility as
the deadlines are missed).

Aiming at assisting project managers in their tasks,
numerous software development effort estimation
techniques have been developed over the last three
decades [1]. In a recent systematic review [2], Jorgensen
and Shepperd identified 304 papers published in 76
journals dealing with the development and the
improvement of software effort estimation approaches
(more details can be found in [2]). These latter are based
on different techniques varying from expert judgment [3]
and statistical analysis [4] to artificial intelligence tools

[5].
In recent years, Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs)
have been emerged as successful modeling techniques
when seeking an approximation of a nonlinear
relationship between the independent and dependent
variables. Previously, Hornik et al. [6] have proved that
ANN are capable to model any measurable function. In
the context of software effort estimation, artificial neural
networks have been increasingly employed to model the

complex relationship linking the software attributes and
software development effort [7]-[8]-[9]. The most used
architecture network in the aforementioned references is
the multilayer perceptron (MLP). Unlike these latter, this
paper uses Radial Basis Function (RBFN) neural
networks to address estimation effort issue.

The use of Radial Basis Function Networks to predict
software development effort requires the determination
of some parameters. In an earlier work [10], we have
empirically evaluated the use of some crisp clustering
techniques in designing the middle layer of RBFN for
software effort estimation. Particularly, the two used
crisp clustering techniques were the K-means and the
APC-III algorithms. The two studies were based on the
COCOMOâ81 and a web hypermedia dataset that
contains 53 software projects. We have found that the
RBFN designed with the K-means algorithm performs
better, in terms of effort estimates accuracy, than the
RBFN designed with APC-III algorithm for the two
datasets.
The purpose of the present paper is to assess and to
discuss the use of fuzzy clustering techniques, especially
the fuzzy C-means algorithm in designing RBFN
networks for software effort estimation. Instead of crisp
(no fuzzy) clustering techniques where each software
project belongs to exactly one cluster, fuzzy clustering
techniques will allow to each software project to belong
to more than one cluster with different degrees. Hence,
the designed model may handle correctly the imprecision
and the uncertainty when assigning a software project to
clusters. This study uses 53 web projects from Tukutuku
database [11].

The rest of this paper is structured as follows: Section
2 provides some background information about the effort
estimation literature. It is followed in Section 3 by a brief
description of RBF Networks. In Section 4, we describe
how fuzzy logic is incorporated in the hidden layer of the
RBFN networks by using fuzzy C-means. A description
of historical projects dataset and evaluation criteria is
presented in Section 5. Experiment design and results
obtained are addressed in Section 6 and 7 respectively. In
Section 8, the concluding remarks are stated and the
future work is discussed.

**II.** **Literature Review**

Although the several software effort estimation
models developed using a broad range of techniques and
tools, accurate and reliable estimates are still crucial goal.
Moreover, the problem has been shown to be a complex
issue and the results obtained have not in general been
encouraging enough.

Both SLIM and COCOMO models are based on the

estimated software size to derive an estimate for the

effort, and yet it is difficult to predict accurately the
required effort at an early stage. To address this issue,
Abrecht developed *function point* method [14] which
consists in the measure of the function that the software
is to perform. The *function* is quantified as *function*
*point*, essentially, a weighted sum of the number of
inputs, outputs, master files, and inquiries provided, or
generated by the software. Albrecht states that *function*
*point* measure is thought to be more useful than SLOC as
a prediction of effort because function points are
relatively easily estimated from a statement of basic
requirements for a program early in the development
cycle [14].

Despite their popularity, algorithmic models suffer
from serious drawbacks [15]. First of all, they are unable
to capture the complex relationship between effort and
software development environment since they are based
on the estimated size (SLOC, FPAâĶ) as the major
determinant of effort. In addition, algorithmic models can
only be calibrated manually. As a matter of fact, Kemerer

[15] performed an empirical validation of the above
algorithmic models using data from projects outside the
original model development environments without
recalibrating the models. The results indicate that the
models developed in different environments do not work
very well when they are uncalibrated, as might be
expected. Average error rates calculated using the MRE
formula ranged from 85 to 772 percent.

*II.2.* *Machine Learning Based Estimation Models*

The problems encountered when using algorithmic
models have emphasized many researchers to look for
new techniques to solve the software effort estimation
issue. In this respect, machine learning methods have
shown to be a promising alternative to the algorithmic
models [16]. These methods focus on learning from the
past projects to provide estimations for the future
projects. In other words, they attempt to approximate the
required effort without making any assumption about the
form of the function linking the effort and the software
attributes. The most machine learning approaches
investigated in the literature are: case-based reasoning,
decision trees, artificial neural networks, and genetic
programming [2].

Case based reasoning is the process of identifying one
or more historical projects that are similar to the project
under development and then generating the estimates
from them. Shepperd and Scofield developed Angel, a
case based reasoning approach to estimate the software
effort [17]. They validated the results in nine different
datasets and showed that estimation by analogy produces a
superior predictive performance in all used datasets when
measured by MMRE and in seven out of nine datasets for
the Pred(25) indicator. In [18], the authors propose a fuzzy
version of case based reasoning to handle imprecise and
uncertain data.

Decision tree is a flow-chart-like hierarchical tree

structure which is composed of three basic elements:
decision nodes corresponding to software project attributes,
edges or branches which correspond to the different possible
software attribute values. The third component is leaves
including projects that typically belong to the same class or
that are very similar. A decision tree can be built using one
of the several induction algorithms developed [19].
Previously, Porter and Selby have used ID3 algorithm to
induce a decision tree for estimating software development
effort and other resource-related dimensions [20]-[21].
Srinivasan and Fisher experiment the use of CARTX to
construct a decision tree [5]. The results showed possible
advantages of this approach over traditional models. Other
non-standard decision trees were proposed. Namely, fuzzy
decision trees [22]-[23] which blend decision trees with
fuzzy set tools to manage fuzzy information (attribute and
class values are vaguely expressed with linguistic fuzzy
terms).

Genetic programming is a variation on the genetic
algorithms, in which the representation of the
chromosomes is not a binary string but other data
structures. For instance in [24], the software effort
equations are treated as chromosomes. Initially, a number of
random effort equations are generated. Afterwards, a new
population of effort equations is created from previous
effort equations by applying genetic operations to the most
accurate equations until an accurate enough effort equation
is reached. Several relative works indicate that genetic


programming can offer significant improvements in the
effort estimation accuracy [25]-[26]-[27].

Recently, artificial neural networks have attracted
numerous researchers due to their approximation and
learning abilities. In software effort estimation issue, they
have become an important alternative approach to
algorithmic models [28]. The first study concerned the use
of ANNs to predict software development effort were
undertaken by Hsu and Tenorio [29]. They used Multilayer
Perceptron (MLP) constructed using self organizing neural
network and backpropagation algorithms. Their preliminary
results showed that ANN models have been superior in
performance, learning time, modeling power, and require
fewer prior assumptions than traditional software effort
estimation models. In [5], the authors compared two
approaches (1) a back propagation neural network and (2)
regression trees, using COCOMO database. Their
experimental results showed that neural network and
regression trees are competitive with algorithmic based
models. Moreover, using a dataset from Australian Software
Metrics Association, ANN estimation models showed to be
more accurate, in term of MARE, than CBR estimation
models [30].

In [8], Heiat compared neural network estimation
method to regression approach for software effort
estimation. The results of his comparison indicate that
neural network approach was competitive with regression
when third generation language data set was used in the
study. However, in his second study when a combined
third and fourth generation language data set were used,
the computed Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE)
of the neural network model was significantly smaller
than MAPE of regression model.

Kumar al al. used wavelet neural network to forecast
the software development effort [9]. Also, they have
compared the effectiveness of their proposed WNN
model to other types of neural network and traditional
models. The study was based on Canadian Financial
dataset and IBM data processing services dataset. Their
results suggest that wavelet neural network outperformed
all the other techniques in terms of mean magnitude
relative error (MMRE).

However, Neural networks estimation models are
complex with too many parameters to adjust. Hence,
relatively large datasets are required for accurate and
stable estimates. Furthermore, they are sensitive to
training data, prone to get stuck in local minimums [5]
[31]. To overcome these drawbacks, Kultur et al.
proposed the use of the ensemble of neural networks with
associative memory (ENNA) [31]. In this latter model an
ensemble is used rather than a single MLP. When a new
project is given to the model for estimation, each MLP
provides its own result. These results are clustered and
the average of the largest cluster is returned. Thereafter,
associative memory is used for bias correction.

**VIII.** **Conclusion and Future Work**

In this paper, we have empirically studied the use of
two clustering techniques when designing RBF neural
network for software effort estimation. The two used
clustering algorithms are the well-known hard and the
fuzzy C-means. This study is based on one dataset
containing 53 web applications. We used the entire
dataset to train and test the designed RBFN. We have
found that the RBFN designed with the fuzzy C-means
algorithm performs better, in terms of the effort estimates
accuracy, than the RBFN designed with the hard Kmeans algorithm. To confirm this result, we are looking
currently in applying cross validation methods to
compare the generalization ability of hard and fuzzy Cmeans based RBFN for software development effort
estimation.
