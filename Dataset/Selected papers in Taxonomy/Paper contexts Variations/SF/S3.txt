# Investigating Metrics for a Development Effort Prediction Model of Web Applications

Emilia Mendes

*Computer Science Department*
*The University of Auckland*

*[emilia@cs.auckland.ac.nz](mailto:emilia@cs.auckland.ac.nz)*


Abstract

Although in the hypermedia literature there are metrics
proposed to measure hypermedia processes and
products, many lack the necessary theoretical and
empirical validation.
To address these issues, this paper presents the results
of a quantitative case study which validated empirically
a set of metrics proposed to measure the development
effort involved in authoring Web applications. These
metrics adhere to the representational theory of

measurement.

The results obtained for the case study have shown
that three out of four of the proposed metrics
presented statistically significant correlations with
development effort, suggesting that they may be useful
parameters for a prediction model that estimates the
effort involved in developing Web applications.

1. Introduction

Developing good quality hypermedia applications
has a high cost, mostly in time and the amount of
difficulty involved for the authors [4]. Therefore, if we
know more about how those applications are
developed, we can have better control over them and,
consequently, the chances of improving the
development will increase. To facilitate control,
measurement and feedback are obtained as the first

step to improve products, processes and resources.

As engineering involves the analysis of
measurements [18], hypermedia engineering and Web
engineering, will


only become true engineering disciplines when a solid
foundation of measurement-based theories is built.

Although there is some research into hypermedia
metrics, most of it to date uses an analytical method of
research without further empirical justification.
Consequently, many of the published research findings
can only be interpreted as analytical advocacy research

[8].

The main objective of this research project was to
investigate the process of Web authoring by using a
scientific method of research, which comprised the
following steps:

i) The proposal of metrics to measure the
maintainability of Web applications, the reusability of
Web documents and the development effort involved
in authoring a Web application.

ii) The proposal of metrics which adhere to the
representational theory of measurement.

iii) The empirical and theoretical validation of
our metrics according to the validation framework
proposed by Kitchenham et al. [12].

The first part of our research project was a survey,
which measured the maintainability of hypermedia
applications and the reusability of hypermedia
information [15]. This paper presents the second part
of the project - a quantitative case study aimed at
validating empirically our proposed metrics as possible
measures of the development effort involved in
authoring Web applications. In Section 2, the
quantitative case study is described. Section 3 presents
the validity of the case study evaluation and the
theoretical validation of the proposed metrics
described in Section 4. Finally, in Section 5, the results
of the case study are presented and in Section 6 we
give our conclusions and comments on future work.

2.1 Design of the Evaluation

Two questionnaires were used in the case study: the
first one - Q1 - asked subjects to rate their level of
Web authoring experience on a scale from 1 (little
experience) to 10 (a great deal of experience); the
second one - Q2 - (see Appendix A) was used to
measure the independent and dependent variables.
Both questionnaires were checked by members of the
research group for ambiguous questions, unusual tasks,
definitions in the appendix and the number of
questions.

The subjects who participated in the evaluation
were 45 second-year Computer Science students
attending the Human-Computer Interaction course. An
analysis of the questionnaires obtained showed that six
were missing fundamental information. Consequently,
these samples were not used in the data analysis.

Subjects were given a coursework that consisted of
developing Web applications to teach about HumanComputer Interaction concepts. They were also
required to structure their applications according to the
Cognitive Flexibility Theory (CFT) >22] principles,
and to create the applications using a minimum of 50
documents.

In order to reduce any learning effects
(confounding factors) >11] subjects were given an
initial task - to develop their personal Web pages Two Web tutorials were suggested so that it would be
possible for subjects with no Web authoring
experience to learn how to develop Web pages. In
addition, all the subjects received training on the CFT
authoring principles (90 minutes).

The questionnaire Q2 was handed in together with
the coursework (the Web application). This
questionnaire had two sections: one to collect
attributes of the application and another to collect the
elapsed time spent in developing the application. The
development was split in several sub-tasks along the
development life-cycle, adapted from existing
literature >1,9,10,13,14,17,20,21,23] and organised as
follows: i) Structure the application; ii) Create the text
contents; iii) Create the graphics contents; iv) Create
the video contents; v) Create the audio contents; vi)
Create the links; vii) Design the interface: planning of
the interface; viii) Design the interface: implementing
of the interface; ix) Test the application: test the links
in the application; and x) Test the application: testing
the media.


Each of these subtasks would correspond to an
elapsed time, which would be correlated statistically
with the independent variables in order to check the
hypotheses proposed.

2.2 Dependent and Independent Variables

The independent variables (metrics) used in the
case study evaluation were: hyperdocument size,
connectivity, perceived compactness and perceived
stratum ; The dependent variable used to measure the
development effort was the estimated elapsed time it
took each subject to develop the Web application. At
this stage of the research our concern was with the
measurement for assessment rather than a

measurement for prediction >7]. This means that we
were interested in understanding the development
effort involved in authoring Web applications and how
the independent variables were related to it, rather than
producing a mathematical model (prediction model) to
be used as a predictor of development effort.

Hyperdocument size refers to the number of
documents that each Web application has. Documents
are HTML files, and a Web application is a collection
of documents designed with a specific aim. In our
case, the domain considered to be representative was
education. The Web applications considered are
'static', where the number of dynamically generated
pages is quite small, if not absent.

Connectivity refers to the number of links that the
Web application has. The links considered here are
either structural or referential links >5], leaving apart
dynamically computed links.

Compactness >3] indicates how inter-connected the
documents are. A completely connected application
means that, from each document, there are links
reaching all the other documents in the application.
Compactness was measured in the case study
evaluation by asking subjects to give an estimate of it,
from 1 (completely disconnected) to 5 (completely
connected). Consequently, the compactness measured
was the perceived compactness ( P-Compactness ).

Stratum >3] indicates to what degree the Web
application is organised for directed reading. A
sequential application represents an application where
there is only one path to be followed by the reader. For
the case study evaluation, subjects were asked to
estimate the stratum of the application, from 1 (no
sequential navigation) to 5 (sequential navigation).

Consequently, the stratum measured was the perceived
stratum ( P-Stratum ).

2.3 The Hypotheses

Although in a scientific method of research the
hypotheses to be tested should be the null hypotheses,
we decided to present the alternative hypotheses as
they explicitly show the direction of the relationship
between the independent and dependent variables. Our
stated hypotheses for the evaluation were as follows:

H1 - The bigger the hyperdocument size the longer
it takes to develop the Web application.
H2 - The greater the connectivity the longer it takes
to develop the Web application.
H3 - The higher the p-compactness the longer it
takes to develop the Web application.
H4 - The smaller the p-stratum the longer it takes to
develop the Web application.

3. Validity of the Case Study Evaluation

There are three types of threats to the validity of an
empirical study >11]:

- Construct validity: Represents to what extent the
independent and dependent variables precisely
measure the concepts they claim to measure.

- Internal validity: Represents to what extent
conclusions can be drawn about the casual effect

of the independent variables on the dependent
variables.

- External validity: Represents the domain to which
a study's findings can be generalised.

3.1 Construct Validity

it would be very time-consuming. Compactness,

Finally, stratum, as proposed by Botafogo et al.

Therefore, we decided to re-use these concepts

aware that there are other metrics which can also

be used (for example, the length of the
application, the complexity of documents and the
functionality offered) and they will be the focus of
our next evaluation.

3.2 Internal Validity

The following problems might threaten the internal
validity of the experiment:

- There were three confounding factors in this
evaluation: i) subjects' experience; ii) maturation
effects, which are learning effects caused by
subjects learning as an experiment proceeds; and
iii) tools used to help develop/manage the Web
application. The data collected showed that: i) the
experiences did vary greatly. Consequently, it was
decided to consider two separate groups   - subjects
with experience levels ranging from 1 to 5 (22
subjects)   - LEL group - and subjects with
experience levels ranging from 6 to 10 (17
subjects)   - HEL group; ii) subjects were asked to
develop their Web pages before developing the
applications and they all received training in the
CFT principles. In addition, they were given a
general structure for their applications, using the
CFT. Consequently, it seems that the maturation
effects were controlled, or at least reduced; and
iii) the number of tools used did not vary greatly.
This result was confirmed statistically.

- The selection effects, which are due to natural
variations in subject performance, were
controlled.

- The instrumentation effects in general did not
occur in this evaluation. The questionnaires used
were the same and the same hypermedia system
was used to develop the applications.

3.3 External Validity

The following problems might limit the external
validity of the experiment:

- The results may be domain dependent as all the
subjects answered the questionnaires based on

their experience in developing Web applications
for education. This evaluation should therefore be

repeated in domains other than education if the
results are to be generalised to other domains.

- The experimental scale >19] is a threat when the
experimental setting or the materials are not
representative of industrial practice. The
applications developed presented good quality
interface and contents, when compared to other
Web applications found in industry. Therefore,
from this perspective it seems that the applications
developed are representative of industrial practice.

- The applications did not have more than 100
documents and 300 links, on average, as the
subjects were supposed to use only three working
days to develop them. These numbers represent
small applications and are probably not
representative of the large Web applications for
education developed by some organisations, in
general. On the other hand, the applications
developed had a similar to or even better interface
and contents quality than Web applications
developed by professionals, as noticed by myself
when marking the Web applications developed by
the students. These results seem to indicate that

the results of the case study are likely to scale-up.

- A threat to subject generalizability >19] may exist
when the subject population is not drawn from the
wider population. The subjects that participated in
the case study may not be representative of Web
professionals, since second-year Computer
Science students cannot be categorised as
experienced Web authors. The use of students as
subjects, while sometimes considered unrealistic,
is justified for two reasons: firstly, empirical
evidence by Bohem-Davis and Ross (1992)
indicates that students are equal to professionals in
many quantifiable measures, including their
approach to developing software; secondly, for
pragmatic considerations, having students as
subjects was the only viable option for this case
study.

3. Theoretical Validation of the Proposed
Metrics

This Section presents a theoretical validation of our
metrics according to the validation framework
proposed by Kitchenham et al. >12].


For the validation framework, to decide whether a
measurement is valid it is necessary to confirm:

- Attribute validity, i.e., whether the attribute is
actually exhibited by the entity being measured.

- Unit validity, i.e., whether the measurement unit
being used is an appropriate means of measuring
the attribute.

- Instrument validity, i.e., whether any model
underlying a measuring instrument is valid and the
measuring instrument is properly calibrated.

- Protocol validity, i.e., whether an acceptable
measurement protocol is adopted.

The properties of measures used in the validation are:
(1) For an attribute to be measurable, it must allow
different entities to be distinguished from one
another.

(2) A valid measure must obey the representation
condition, preserving our intuitive notions about
the attribute and the way in which it distinguishes
different entities.

(3) Each unit of an attribute contributing to a valid
measure is equivalent.
(4) Different entities can have the same attribute
value.

4.1 Validation of Instrument Models

Validating a measurement instrument represents the
confirmation that the instrument accurately measures
attribute values in a given unit. The instrument used in
the quantitative case study measured all the attributes

3. Conclusions

This paper has reported the findings of a
quantitative case study aimed at empirically validating
proposed metrics. The case study involved the
development of Web applications using the principles
of the Cognitive Flexibility Theory. The data was
collected using questionnaires, which were answered
by the subjects who participated in the case study.

Results have shown that three of the four metrics

proposed - hyperdocument size, connectivity and pcompactness - have presented statistically significant
correlations with development effort, suggesting that
these metrics could be used in the future as parameters
of an effort prediction model of Web development.

An important lesson learnt from the use of
measurement is that it is better to start from a set of

metrics addressing important improvement areas, and
evolve these metrics over time, instead of debating
forever, trying to find perfect metrics [6]. We believe
that the steps used to propose and validate the metrics
were as important as, or even more important than, the
metrics themselves.

As part of our future work we are currently
developing a tool that measures our proposed metrics
automatically. Our aim is to use this tool in our next
evaluation, which will be a replication of the case
study presented in this paper. We hope that the results
obtained from the replication study will give us the


necessary feedback towards the definition of an effort
prediction model of Web development.

can easily reach, using links, any other document in the application, suggesting a large amount of cross-referencing.
