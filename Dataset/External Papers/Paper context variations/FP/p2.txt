**1. Introduction**


A B S T R A C T

Effort estimation plays a vital role in software project planning, as accurate estimates of required human re-­
sources are essential for success. Traditional estimation models often depend on historical size and effort data,
yet organizations frequently struggle to access reliable effort records. Public benchmarking datasets like ISBSG
offer useful data but may lack coverage or involve licensing fees. To address this issue, we previously introduced
a free, extendable benchmarking dataset that integrates functional size and effort data extracted from 18 studies.
In this study, we examine the effectiveness of our dataset for predictive effort estimation and compare it with the
widely used ISBSG dataset. Our analysis includes 337 records from our dataset and 732 ISBSG projects, focusing
on those with COSMIC size data. We first developed and compared models using linear regression and nine
machine learning algorithms — Bayesian Ridge, Ridge Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost,
LightGBM, k-Nearest Neighbors, Multi-Layer Perceptron, and Support Vector Regression. Then, we selected the
best-performing models and applied them to an unseen evaluation dataset to assess their generalization per-­
formance. The results show that machine learning performance varies based on evaluation method and dataset
characteristics. Despite having fewer records, our dataset enabled more accurate predictions than ISBSG in most
cases, highlighting its potential for effort estimation. This study demonstrates the viability of our dataset for
building predictive models and supports the use of machine learning in improving estimation accuracy.
Expanding this dataset could offer a valuable, open-access resource for organizations seeking effective and lowcost estimation solutions.

A software development project is considered successful when it
fulfills its customers’ expectations and is delivered on the agreed time
and budget (Standish Group Chaos Report, 2020). Therefore, in project
management, effort estimation is important in accurately predicting the
required human resource, which is the most fundamental resource in
software projects (Agrawal and Chari, 2007; Gencel and Demirors,
2008). In most cases, two primary inputs are utilized to create an effort
prediction model: historical size and effort data. The size of the software
project can be measured at different stages of the project, from the re-­
quirements, code, or user interface. However, accessing historical effort
data from previous projects may not always be possible within organi-­
zations. Therefore, it can be challenging to create an estimation model
(Ozkaya et al., 2011) [¨] . In such cases, software organizations may use
software benchmarking datasets to construct effort estimation models or

The general approach to effort estimation in the literature is to filter
projects from an organization’s past projects or benchmarking datasets
like ISBSG and build a regression-based model. Many studies have
developed predictive effort estimation models using this approach, and
software organizations have achieved success in project planning with
the regression-based models they created (Abran et al., 2015; Hacaloglu ˘
et al., 2024; Salmanoglu et al., 2017; Ünlü et al., 2023). However, in
today’s software world, machine learning algorithms, which are used for
various purposes (Al-Sabbagh et al., 2022; Bergstrom et al., 2022¨ ), have
a huge potential to perform prediction tasks (Giray, 2021; Polkowski
et al., 2019; Zhang and Tsai, 2003). These algorithms can provide a
more precise estimation of effort.

In the literature, different studies attempted to evaluate the success
of prediction models using various functional size measures as input.
However, to the best of our knowledge, no study has gathered the
functional size and effort data from the literature. In our previous study
(Yürüm et al., 2024), we aimed to answer whether creating a useful
software benchmark dataset is possible by gathering the data from the
literature, thus addressing the gaps in existing datasets. We formed a
dataset including 337 records from 18 studies that shared the related
size and effort data. Although we performed a limited search, we created
a larger dataset than many datasets (excluding ISBSG) in the literature
(Bosu and Macdonell, 2019). In summary, we provided a reliable, free,
and extendable software benchmarking dataset derived from scientific
studies.

Although benchmarking datasets serve various purposes, they are
primarily used for effort estimation when an organization lacks histor-­
ical data. Numerous studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of the

ISBSG dataset in this context. However, can the dataset we created also
support predictive effort estimation? This study aims to answer that
question. Building on the promising results of our previous study, where
we explored the feasibility of constructing a software benchmarking
dataset through a systematic literature review, we expanded our
research with two key questions: (1) Can our dataset enable predictive
effort estimation? (2) How can a machine-learning-based approach
improve estimation precision?

For this purpose, we developed effort estimation models using 337
projects from our dataset and 732 projects from the ISBSG repository
that include COSMIC size data. We categorized productivity levels as
high and low, dividing the data into two separate groups. For each
group, in addition to traditional linear regression, we applied nine ma-­
chine learning algorithms: Bayesian Ridge, Ridge Regression, Decision
Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN),
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), and Support Vector Regression (SVM).
These algorithms represent a broad range of predictive approaches,
including linear, tree-based, instance-based, neural network-based, and
kernel-based methods. After training and validating the models using
LOOCV, we selected the best-performing algorithms and evaluated their
generalization ability on unseen test data.

The results demonstrated that machine learning algorithms enabled
better predictive effort estimation. While the effort estimation model
built with our dataset did not always yield a statistically significant
change, it provided better predictive results compared to the model
based on the ISBSG dataset. The findings of this extended study further
validate the dataset’s potential. Despite having fewer projects than
ISBSG, it facilitated a better predictive effort estimation model.
Expanding the dataset could further enhance domain-specific models
and offer organizations free access to a valuable resource for effort

estimation.

In summary, the contributions of this study are as follows. First, it
builds upon our previous study, where we created a free, extendable
software benchmarking dataset derived from 337 records across 18
studies. Second, it demonstrates that this dataset can be used for pre-­
dictive effort estimation, showing its potential compared to the ISBSG
dataset. Third, the study applies a variety of machine learning


algorithms, including tree-based and neural network-based methods, to
improve the accuracy of effort estimation over traditional linear
regression models.

The paper is structured as follows: Section 2 covers the related work,
Section 3 details the research methodology, Section 4 presents the re-­
sults, Section 5 discusses findings, including validity threats, and Section
6 concludes with suggestions for further studies.

**2. Related work**

We summarize the related work in the literature in two main cate-­
gories: using ISBSG for effort estimation purposes and using machine
learning in the effort estimation of software projects.

Organizations often rely on ISBSG data to build effort estimation
models when they lack sufficient historical data to create internal
models. Several studies have highlighted the use of ISBSG for effort
estimation in such cases. Ünlü et al. (2021) demonstrated the applica-­
bility of ISBSG data for effort estimation by comparing the estimated and
actual effort of projects, revealing an average error rate of around 15 %.
Mendes et al. (2005) compared effort estimation models using both
organizational historical data and ISBSG data, finding similar error rates
for both. Ozcan-Top et al. (2011) [¨] assessed the effectiveness of external
and multi-organizational software benchmarking repositories,
concluding that ISBSG provided the most accurate results after the or-­
ganization’s own data.

However, like many software engineering datasets, the ISBSG dataset
suffers from missing data, which can affect the accuracy of estimation
models. To address this, Bala and Abran (2018) applied simple and
multiple imputation techniques, finding that simple imputation with a
relative average seed produced the most accurate estimates compared to
a complete dataset. Finally, Gonz´alez-Ladron-de-Guevara et al. ´ [(2016)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?kacxDA)
conducted a literature review of 107 articles using ISBSG data for effort
estimation, identifying size and effort as the most frequently used var-­
iables, with data quality ranking and counting approach variables often
used for filtering.

Machine learning algorithms have great potential for prediction
tasks, making it important to assess popular and effective algorithms for
software effort estimation. These algorithms have been widely used in
various domains for prediction and classification (Afuwape et al., 2021;
Kolukisa and Bakir-Gungor, 2023; Teles et al., 2021). In the field of
software engineering, several studies have examined different machine
learning algorithms for effort estimation, producing varying results
depending on the datasets and algorithms used (Brar and Nandal, 2022).

BaniMustafa, 2018 applied Random Forest for software effort esti-­
mation and found that it achieved higher accuracy than traditional
methods. Goyal and Bhatia (2019) showed that Multi-Layer Perceptron
performed better than linear regression for effort estimation. Lowast and
Kumari, 2020 compared machine learning algorithms and concluded
that Support Vector Machines provided the most accurate results .
Rehman et al., 2021 analyzed several machine learning methods and
found that Decision Trees gave better results in most cases, while Ridge
Regression worked better in some situations . Sahoo et al., 2022 studied
multiple regression techniques and found that Bayesian Ridge Regres-­
sion was the most accurate compared to Decision Tree, Support Vector
Regression, and XGBoost. Natarajan and Balachandran (2022) proposed
an approach using XGBoost that considered risk factors and improved
effort estimation accuracy. Rahman et al. (2023) tested several machine
learning models and found that Decision Trees performed better than
other algorithms such as k-Nearest Neighbors and Support Vector
Regression. Iordan and Covaciu (2023) examined k-Nearest Neighbors
and found it useful for predicting effort. Demir et al., 2023 investigated
LightGBM and confirmed its reliability for effort estimation . Venka-­
traman et al. (2024) showed that Random Forest had better performance
than other ensemble learning methods. Lastly, Ünlü et al., 2025
compared linear regression with six machine learning algorithms for
data warehouse projects in the ISBSG repository and found that Decision

**3. Research methodology**

The research methodology of this study consists of two main steps:
the construction of the dataset conducted in our previous study and
effort estimation with machine learning carried out in this study.

*3.1. Construction of the dataset*

In the construction of the benchmarking dataset step, we followed
the standard process for conducting a systematic literature review
(Kitchenham, 2004). The details of the steps performed can be found in
our previous study (Yürüm et al., 2024). The process involved defining
research objectives and questions, performing pilot searches, and con-­
structing a search string focused on COSMIC size measurement. We used
Scopus as the primary database and applied specific inclusion and
exclusion criteria to filter relevant studies. An initial search yielded 548
publications, which were refined through title, abstract, and full-text
screening, ultimately selecting 18 studies. Data extraction and synthe-­
sis were conducted collaboratively, ensuring accuracy and consistency
for further analysis.

*3.2. Effort estimation with machine learning algorithms*

Effort estimation includes the procedures employed to develop ma-­
chine learning models for software effort estimation. These procedures
are explained in five subsections: data preprocessing, model develop-­
ment, model evaluation, performance indicators and statistical
comparison.

*3.2.1. Data preprocessing*

Prior to model development, rigorous preprocessing steps were
applied to ensure data quality and suitability for machine learning:

 - Projects were segmented into low-productivity and highproductivity groups based on mean productivity values.

 - Logarithmic transformation was applied to functional size and effort
to improve distributional symmetry and compress scale.

 Outliers were identified and removed to retain only representative
project data and enhance model reliability.

*3.2.2. Model development*

A comprehensive suite of ten machine learning algorithms was
implemented to predict software effort. The selection encompassed a
broad spectrum of techniques, including:

 - Linear Approaches: Linear Regression, Bayesian Ridge, and Ridge
Regression

 - Tree-Based Methods: Decision Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost and
LightGBM

 - Instance-Based Learning: k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

 - Neural Networks: Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)

 - Kernel-Based Methods: Support Vector Regression (SVM)


Model training was performed using a Leave-One-Out Cross-Vali-­
dation (LOOCV) scheme, which maximized the use of available data and
provided an unbiased assessment of predictive performance.

*3.2.3. Model evaluation*

Following model development, model evaluation was performed
using a dedicated subset of held-out project instances, referred to as the
shared evaluation dataset. The construction of this dataset is detailed in

Section 4.2. All entries in the shared set were excluded from training and
validation to ensure unbiased assessment of generalization performance.

After identifying the best-performing model for each productivity
group via Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV), these models were
applied to the shared dataset. No further training or tuning was con-­
ducted during this evaluation phase. This procedure allowed us to assess
how well the selected models transferred to previously unseen data
under consistent conditions.

Performance results obtained from this evaluation were used in

conjunction with the metrics described in Section 3.2.4 and subjected to
statistical comparison as outlined in Section 3.2.5.

*3.2.5. Statistical comparison*

Finally, we conducted a Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test to compare the
predictive performance of the top-performing models trained on our
dataset and the ISBSG dataset. The Wilcoxon test is a nonparametric
alternative to the paired *t* -test, suitable for evaluating differences be-­
tween two related samples without assuming normality (Wilcoxon,
1945; Hollander, Wolfe, and Chicken, 2013). This makes it appropriate
for our analysis, as the distribution of the differences between paired
prediction errors deviates from normality. In our implementation, we
used the *scipy.stats.wilcoxon* function from the SciPy library (Virtanen
et al., 2020) to analyze the absolute prediction errors generated on the
shared evaluation dataset. By comparing the residuals from models
trained on each dataset, we assessed whether the observed differences in
predictive performance were statistically significant.

**4. Results**

This section introduces the constructed dataset, and the shared
evaluation dataset derived from ISBSG and our dataset, followed by the
results of effort estimation using machine learning techniques.

*4.1. Dataset*

As a result of our systematic literature review, we formed a dataset
including 337 records from 18 studies that shared the corresponding size
and effort data. The following sections summarize the dataset and
compare it with the ISBSG dataset.

*4.1.1. Dataset summary*

We constructed the dataset based on 11 features derived from the

ISBSG repository features and the provided case details of the studies. As
many studies lack some ISBSG features, we could not include all features
of the ISBSG dataset. These features are demonstrated in Table 1, which
includes their descriptions and some examples.

In the distribution of development types in the dataset, most studies
used effort and size data from newly developed projects, while
enhancement projects were less common. In terms of effort units,
person-hour was the most commonly used, followed by person-day in
nearly half of the studies and person-month in a few. Due to the varia-­
tion in effort units, normalized effort, defined as person-hour, was
derived. Additionally, each project’s domain/type was analyzed,
revealing that most projects were categorized as management infor-­
mation systems or application systems, and the dataset also included
projects from various domains such as telecommunication, finance,
banking, and defense.

Productivity is defined as the ratio of normalized effort (in personhours) to functional size (in COSMIC Function Points. Although pro-­
ductivity is generally defined in the literature as output/input (e.g., FP/
hour), in this study we followed the ISBSG convention and calculated
productivity as input/output (e.g., hours/FP), also known as Project
Delivery Rate (PDR). This approach was adopted to ensure consistency

*4.1.2. Comparison with ISBSG*

We compared its descriptive statistics with those of the ISBSG COSMIC dataset to assess the characteristics of our dataset. This com-­
parison focuses on three key aspects: project size, effort, and produc-­
tivity (see Table 2, Table 3, and Table 4).

Our dataset includes a variety of project sizes, with a smaller overall
range compared to the ISBSG dataset. The average project size in our
dataset is lower than those in the ISBSG repository. Furthermore, our
dataset exhibits less variability in project size, suggesting a more
consistent distribution of project types. When comparing effort, our
dataset shows a lower average effort than the ISBSG dataset. This dif-­
ference is accompanied by a smaller median and a less pronounced
variation in effort, which reflects a more uniform distribution of effort
across the projects in our dataset.

Regarding productivity, our dataset reveals less variation than the
ISBSG repository. Projects in our dataset show a narrower range of
productivity values, indicating that productivity is more concentrated
around the average. This contrasts with the broader spread of produc-­
tivity values in the ISBSG dataset, which may be influenced by the in-­
clusion of larger and more complex projects. There may be two reasons
behind this: (1) The ISBSG repository’s mean size is greater than our
dataset’s mean size. Therefore, the complexity of large projects may
result in low productivity, and that might increase the variation. (2)
Many studies we used to create our dataset could have lacked support for
SDLC activities, such as project management, configuration manage-­
ment, and reviews, including in the given effort. Therefore, if these
projects only include partial SDLC activities, the reported effort may be
lower than actual, resulting in higher productivity values.

*4.2. Shared dataset from ISBSG and our dataset*

To enable a fair and consistent comparison of model performance,
we constructed a shared evaluation dataset comprising 19 unique
project entries. These entries were selected based on matching values of
the two core variables used in effort estimation: (1) Size and (2) Effort.
Each matched pair was present in both our dataset and the ISBSG re-­
pository. The shared dataset was created by performing an inner join on
these two variables across the datasets.

To prevent any form of data leakage during model training and
evaluation, we removed all occurrences of these 19 shared entries from
both datasets prior to model training. As a result, the filtered versions of
our dataset and ISBSG included 315 and 704 projects respectively,
comprising only non-overlapping observations. This approach ensured
that the models trained on each dataset were evaluated on an identical

set of previously unseen instances, allowing for a direct and statistically
meaningful comparison of their generalization performance.

This shared evaluation dataset is later used for assessing model
performance and comparing predictive outcomes between our dataset
and ISBSG.

*4.3. Effort estimation with machine learning algorithms*

Effort estimation using machine learning in our study is divided into
two main stages: (1) Data Preprocessing and (2) Effort Estimation. In the
following subsections, we detail each stage, outlining the individual
steps and presenting the results obtained at each stage.

*4.3.1. Data preprocessing*

Data preprocessing is a crucial step in effort estimation as it ensures
that the dataset is clean, well-structured, and suitable for machine
learning models. This stage involves segmenting projects based on
productivity levels, applying log transformation to normalize the data,
and identifying and removing outliers that may seriously affect esti-­
mations. These preprocessing steps enhance data quality, reduce bias,
and improve the predictive performance of the models.

*4.3.1.1. Productivity-based division.* Effort estimation accuracy can vary
significantly based on the productivity levels of software development
projects. To account for this variability, the dataset was segmented into
two groups based on the mean productivity score. This segmentation
allowed for the development of models that were tailored to the specific
characteristics of each group, thereby improving predictive accuracy.

The productivity score for each project was calculated as the ratio of
actual effort to functional size (CFP). A higher productivity score in-­
dicates that more effort was required per unit of functional size, while a
lower score suggests greater efficiency in effort utilization. The mean
productivity score of the dataset was used as a threshold to divide
projects into the following categories:

1. Low-Productivity Projects: Projects with a productivity score higher
than the mean, indicating greater effort per unit of functional size.
2. High-Productivity Projects: Projects with a productivity score lower
than the mean, indicating more efficient effort utilization.

Descriptive statistics for low and high productivity levels in our dataset.


Once categorized, machine learning models were trained separately
on each group. This approach enabled a more granular analysis of effort
estimation patterns, allowing models to focus on the unique character-­
istics of projects within each productivity category. By distinguishing
between different productivity levels, the models could better capture
variations in software development effort, leading to more precise
predictions.

The productivity-based division categorized 77 projects as low pro-­
ductivity and 238 projects as high productivity in our dataset. In
contrast, ISBSG contained 210 low-productivity projects and 494 high
productivity projects. Table 5 presents the descriptive statistics for low
and high productivity levels in our dataset, while Table 6 provides the
corresponding statistics for ISBSG.

Productivity plays a crucial role in effort estimation, with lower
values indicating greater efficiency - meaning less effort is required to
complete a given amount of work. When comparing Our Dataset with
ISBSG, it becomes clear that our dataset consistently achieves better
productivity, requiring less effort for similar tasks.

*4.3.1.2. Log* *transformation.* Software effort estimation datasets
frequently exhibit right-skewed distributions, where a small number of
projects require substantially higher effort than the majority. To address
this issue, a natural logarithmic transformation was applied to both the
CFP and Effort variables using NumPy’s log function (Harris et al.,

2020
). This transformation significantly improved distributional sym-­
metry and greatly stabilized variance, thereby enhancing the effective-­
ness of the model training process, particularly for linear models. By
compressing extreme values and linearizing exponential relationships
(Benoit, K., 2011), the transformation also facilitated more meaningful
comparisons between linear and non-linear modeling approaches.

*4.3.1.3. Outlier detection and removal.* Although the log transformation
reduced skewness and mitigated the influence of large values, it did not
fully eliminate the effect of extreme cases in the datasets. Some data
points continued to exhibit disproportionately high residuals or leverage
after transformation. Therefore, additional outlier detection and
removal were necessary to improve model stability and ensure more
reliable training and evaluation.

Outliers can significantly impact the performance of machine
learning models by introducing bias and distorting predictions. To
ensure that our effort estimation datasets are both representative and
free from extreme values, we implemented an outlier detection and
removal process based on the Interquartile Range (IQR) method using
Tukey’s Hinges (Beyer, 1981). In this approach, any data point that falls
*>* 1.5 times the IQR below the first quartile (Q₁) or above the third
quartile (Q₃) is flagged as an outlier.

The formulas used are:

Lower Fence = Q 1 − 1.5 × IQR
Upper Fence = Q 3 +1.5 × IQR where IQR= Q 3 -Q 1 .
Based on this formula, one outlier was identified and removed from
each of the low- and high-productivity project groups in our dataset. For
the ISBSG dataset, eight low-productivity and three high-productivity
projects were flagged as outliers and subsequently excluded from
further analysis.

*4.3.2. Model training and evaluation*

Following data preprocessing, a Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation
(LOOCV) strategy (Sreedharan et al., 2023) was used to train and vali-­
date machine learning models for effort estimation. LOOCV was chosen
due to its ability to maximize training data utilization while providing
an unbiased assessment of model performance. In this approach, each
data instance was iteratively treated as the test set while the remaining
data was used for model training.

A diverse set of machine learning models was employed for training,


including Linear Regression, Bayesian Ridge, Ridge Regression, Decision
Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN),
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), and Support Vector Regression (SVM).
These models were selected to cover a broad range of predictive ap-­
proaches, including linear, tree-based, instance-based, neural networkbased and kernel-based methods. The entire modeling process was
implemented in Python using established libraries and is summarized in
the pseudocode provided in Fig. 2.

During each iteration of LOOCV, models were trained using the
transformed training data and then used to predict the effort value for
the test instance. The predictions were recorded, and the process
continued until all instances in the dataset had been used as test cases.

Once predictions were obtained, an inverse transformation was applied
to restore the values to their original scale.

Model performance was evaluated using three widely recognized
error metrics: Mean Magnitude of Relative Error (MMRE), Median
Magnitude of Relative Error (MdMRE), and PRED(30). These metrics
provided insights into the accuracy and reliability of each model,
allowing for a comparative analysis of their effectiveness in effort

*5.1.1. To what extent can a software benchmarking dataset be created via a*
*systematic literature review?*

The findings of our previous study (Yürüm et al., 2024) indicated
that a software benchmarking dataset could be constructed through a
systematic literature review; however, the extent to which this was
feasible was subject to several constraints. The dataset constructed in
this study, consisting of 337 records from 18 studies that provided both
COSMIC functional size and effort data, demonstrated that relevant
project data could be systematically extracted from academic literature.
Despite the search being limited to a single database, the resulting
dataset was larger than many existing benchmarking datasets (Bosu and
Macdonell, 2019), suggesting that leveraging published research was a
viable approach for constructing software benchmarking datasets,
particularly in cases where organizations lacked historical project data.

Nevertheless, several challenges were identified that impacted the

restricted availability of project data within the literature. A significant

the extent to which researchers shared their data and provided
comprehensive descriptions. Despite all these limitations, we achieved
to creation of a benchmarking dataset half the size of the COSMIC-sized
projects in the most comprehensive ISBSG dataset by searching only in
Scopus.

*5.1.2. Can our dataset enable predictive effort estimation?*

Benchmarking datasets are widely used for various purposes, with
one of their primary applications being effort estimation in cases where
organizations lack historical data. While the ISBSG dataset has been
extensively validated for this purpose, the predictive capability of our
constructed dataset remained an open question. This study addressed
this gap by assessing whether our dataset can support predictive effort
estimation. To this end, we developed effort estimation models using
337 projects from our dataset and 732 COSMIC-sized projects from the
ISBSG repository. Based on our previous experience (Ünlü et al., 2025),
we divided projects into two sub-groups based on productivity: high and
low productivity, and created separate effort estimation models for each

group.

The primary objective of this study was to assess the effectiveness of
predictive effort estimation models. It is widely recognized that devel-­
oping an accurate effort estimation model using the ISBSG dataset de-­
pends heavily on proper project filtering. However, since our dataset is
still in its early stages, we constructed effort estimation models for both
the ISBSG dataset and our own dataset without applying any filtering,
except for removing outlier projects and employing a productivity-based
categorization.

The validation results confirmed that the constructed dataset is
suitable for supporting predictive effort estimation. When applied to
low-productivity projects, the dataset enabled consistent and reliable
performance across multiple error metrics, suggesting that it provides a
stable foundation for model development. In contrast, results for highproductivity projects showed greater variability depending on how
performance was measured, indicating that outcomes in this category
are more sensitive to data characteristics and evaluation metrics.

*5.1.3. How can a machine-learning-based approach improve estimation*
*precision?*

A machine-learning-based approach can really boost estimation ac-­
curacy by paying close attention to data preparation, validation, and the
choice of modeling technique. By carefully cleaning the data - removing
outliers, dividing projects into groups based on productivity, and
applying a log transformation to normalize effort and size measurements

- we ensure that the model is built on solid, representative data.
Removing outliers helps eliminate extreme values that might otherwise
distort predictions, while grouping projects by productivity allows the
models to be tailored to the unique characteristics of low- and highproductivity projects. The log transformation further helps by smooth-­
ing out skewed data and making the estimation process more reliable.

Using a Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV) strategy is also a
big advantage. LOOCV makes sure that every single data point is used
for both training and validation, which helps prevent overfitting and
gives a more honest picture of how well the model will perform on new
data.

Machine learning models demonstrated strong predictive capabil-­
ities across both low- and high-productivity projects, with certain al-­
gorithms standing out depending on the productivity group and
evaluation metric. For low-productivity projects, Support Vector
Regression (SVM) consistently outperformed other models in both our
dataset and the ISBSG dataset, yielding the lowest MMRE and MdMRE
values and the highest PRED(30) scores. This highlights SVM’s ability to
provide accurate and robust predictions across varying data character-­
istics. For high-productivity projects, model performance was more
context-dependent. In our dataset, LightGBM achieved the lowest
MMRE, indicating strong average performance, while KNN produced the
lowest MdMRE, suggesting more stable typical-case predictions. In
contrast, within the ISBSG dataset, MLP and Random Forest were the
most effective, excelling in average and median error, respectively.

Importantly, all evaluated machine learning models consistently
outperformed traditional linear regression during Leave-One-Out CrossValidation (LOOCV), across both productivity groups. This consistent
advantage emphasizes the capacity of machine learning approaches to
capture nonlinear patterns and interactions that traditional linear
models may overlook. These findings underscore the importance of
selecting models based on data characteristics and performance objec-­
tives and reinforce the value of machine learning techniques in
advancing the accuracy and robustness of software effort estimation.

*5.2. Threats to validity*

Our study has several validity threats related to the research meth-­
odology followed during dataset construction and the application of


machine learning algorithms for effort estimation. One limitation is that
our search was conducted exclusively in the Scopus database, which
may have led to the omission of relevant studies. However, before
selecting Scopus, we performed pilot searches in other databases,
including Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, Science-­
Direct, and Springer Link, and found that Scopus provided the most
extensive coverage. Therefore, we chose it as our primary source.
Additionally, we did not incorporate snowballing, a key practice in
systematic literature reviews, within the study’s scope. However, our
primary objective was to explore whether a software benchmarking
dataset could be constructed using existing literature. Thus, we focused
on an exploratory search rather than an exhaustive one. Despite these
limitations, our study successfully produced a dataset of substantial size.

Another threat may be related to eliminating outlier projects and
splitting the data into two separate groups based on productivity while
creating the effort estimation model. In the data preprocessing step of
our study, we identified and eliminated outlier projects using statistical
techniques such as the Interquartile Range (IQR) method to ensure that
the dataset contained only meaningful and representative data points,
improving the reliability and stability of the machine learning models
used for effort estimation. When eliminating outlier projects in effort
estimation modeling, maintaining an appropriate balance is crucial:
removing too many projects may reduce the dataset size, thereby
decreasing the model’s reliability. However, in this study, the propor-­
tion of eliminated outlier projects was at most around 10 %, and since
the dataset was sufficiently large, this was not a significant concern.
After eliminating outlier projects, we leveraged our previous experience
(Ünlü et al., 2025) to divide the data into two groups: low and high
productivity. Since this categorization reduced the amount of data in the
model, it was essential to ensure a balanced separation of both
sub-groups. As a result, our dataset included 107 low-productivity
projects and 189 high-productivity projects, while the ISBSG dataset
contained 262 low-productivity projects and 415 high-productivity
projects. Although the categorization was not perfectly balanced, the
number of projects in each sub-group was still considerably high
compared to many studies in the literature, allowing us to build reliable
effort estimation models (Abran et al., 2015; Salmanoglu et al., 2017;
Ünlü et al., 2021).

A further threat related to this productivity-based division is that it
may raise concerns about bias or the requirement for prior knowledge.
However, this classification was performed before model training and
was applied independently within each dataset. The aim was to offer two
baseline models that could help organizations without historical data
select a suitable estimator based on project characteristics. In practice,
organizations often have a reasonable understanding of whether a
project is expected to require more or less effort, for example based on
the domain, technological familiarity, or similarity to previously deliv-­
ered projects. This intention has been explicitly clarified in the manu-­
script to ensure that the productivity split is not misinterpreted as part of
the model evaluation procedure.

Even though our study’s findings related to machine learning are
promising, we recognize certain limitations that could be addressed in
future research. Although the dataset compiled for this study includes
multiple features, only functional size was used as a predictor of effort.
This decision was driven by the limited availability and inconsistency of
contextual information in many of the source studies. Essential attrib-­
utes—such as implementation environment, development methodology,

—
and the scope of SDLC activities were frequently omitted or insuffi-­
ciently described. This lack of standardized reporting made it impossible
to identify a second independent variable that was both consistently
available and shared with the ISBSG dataset. Consequently, our
modeling relied on a single predictor across both datasets to ensure
fairness and comparability.

Size is the most significant measurable aspect of software, and un-­
derstanding its predictive power remains critical for effort estimation
(Abran, 2015). Despite this limitation, machine learning models

produced better predictive performance than linear regression, sug-­
gesting their strength even under minimal feature conditions. As the
software industry matures and develops new measures that are widely
accepted and used in practice, there is no doubt that effort prediction

predictive modeling in future studies, we recommend that empirical
software engineering research adopt standardized data reporting prac-­

Another limitation is our use of default parameters for all machine
learning algorithms. This decision facilitated straightforward compari-­

learning models are highly sensitive to hyperparameter settings, and
systematic hyperparameter tuning has the potential to significantly

machine learning for software effort estimation, and we anticipate that
further refinement of these models will unlock their full potential.

Finally, it is important to clarify that the comparison of machine
learning models in this study was based exclusively on performance
obtained through Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV) using the
training and validation data. Since no hyperparameter tuning was con-­
ducted, LOOCV served not only as a validation technique but also as the
primary basis for assessing and comparing the performance of machine
learning algorithms during model selection. While a shared dataset was
used for evaluating the generalization capability of selected models, it
did not serve as a basis for comparing all algorithms. This approach was
intentionally adopted to prevent data leakage and ensure that model
selection remained unbiased. As a result, all conclusions regarding
model superiority are grounded in LOOCV-based validation results,
which were used to identify the most consistent and accurate algorithms
prior to final evaluation.

**6. Conclusion**

Effort estimation is crucial for software project planning, yet orga-­
nizations often struggle due to the lack of reliable historical effort data.
Benchmarking datasets, such as ISBSG, provide an alternative, but their
applicability can be limited by access restrictions and data variability.
To address this gap, we previously constructed a free and extendable
software benchmarking dataset including 337 records from 18 studies
that shared the related size and effort data. In this study, we investigated
our dataset’s effectiveness in predictive effort estimation and assess its
performance against the widely used ISBSG dataset.

By applying a diverse set of machine learning algorithms, our study
demonstrated that machine learning approaches offer strong predictive
performance across both low- and high-productivity project groups.
Support Vector Regression (SVM) consistently yielded the most accurate
results for low-productivity projects in both our dataset and the ISBSG
dataset. For high-productivity projects, no single algorithm was uni-­
versally superior; LightGBM showed the best average performance in
our dataset, while KNN produced more robust median predictions. In
contrast, MLP and Random Forest emerged as top-performing models
within the ISBSG dataset. These results highlight that the effectiveness of
machine learning algorithms varies depending on dataset characteristics
and project productivity levels, reinforcing the need to select models
based on specific performance goals and data attributes.

Despite the promising results, some limitations remain. The dataset
is still in its early stages, and expanding it with additional records could
further enhance its reliability and applicability. Moreover, optimizing
machine learning models through systematic hyperparameter tuning
could improve their predictive accuracy. Although this study utilized
only size and effort as variables in the models, this decision was made to


ensure compatibility with ISBSG and due to the incomplete reporting of
other features in the source studies. In that regard, future research would
benefit from standardized and well-documented datasets that enable the
inclusion of richer contextual variables.

Importantly, the main contribution of this study is not to outperform
ISBSG in predictive performance, but to provide the community with an
open-source benchmark dataset that supports empirical research on
software effort estimation. Its predictive performance is only one aspect
of its broader practical value. The comparison with ISBSG was intended
to assess the initial potential of the dataset under a shared and realistic
modeling setup. Moreover, the methodology adopted in this study-­
—compiling data through a systematic literature review (SLR)—can be
replicated to construct benchmark datasets in other areas of software
engineering, thereby enabling broader and more diverse empirical
investigations.

In conclusion, this study confirms that an alternative, freely available
software benchmarking dataset can support predictive effort estimation.
With continued expansion and refinement, this dataset has the potential
to serve as a valuable resource for organizations that require reliable
effort estimation models without incurring costs associated with pro-­
prietary datasets like ISBSG.
