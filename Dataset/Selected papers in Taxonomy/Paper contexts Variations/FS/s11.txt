Based on the provided research paper "Web Metrics - Estimating Design and Authoring Effort," here is a thorough analysis and summary of the paper:

**Introduction**: 
The authors aim to explore and predict the effort required for web design and authoring processes. They focus on the classification proposed by Lowe and Hall, distinguishing between authoring and design efforts. The former involves managing content and structure, while the latter concerns creating the structure and functionality of the application.

**Methodology**:

1.  Case Study Evaluation: The researchers conducted a case study involving 43 computer science students at the University of Auckland, who created educational web applications. Students' experiences and perceptions regarding web design and authoring were captured through questionnaires.
2.  Data Collection: Various metrics were gathered to assess web application size from different angles – length and complexity. These metrics included product length, connectivity, cyclomatic complexity, and various measures of multimedia usage.
3.  Statistical Techniques Used: Linear regression and stepwise multiple regression algorithms were employed to create prediction models for predicting web design and authoring effort.

**Key Findings**:

1.  Prediction Process Overview: The prediction process includes collecting historical data, selecting relevant metrics, applying appropriate analytical techniques, evaluating the models, and implementing the final prediction models.
2.  Identified Factors Influencing Web Development Effort: Several factors affecting web development effort were identified, including experience level, maturity level, structural style, and tool usage.
3.  Results of Empirical Investigation: Significant findings revealed statistically significant correlations between certain metrics like total page allocation, total code length, connectivity, and effort.

**Summary of Key Points**:

1.  **Metrics Collected:** Twenty-five metrics were extracted across six categories: length-size, reusability, complexity-size, effort, and confounding factors.
2.  **Length-Sized Metrics:** Twelve metrics measured aspects such as page count, media count, and total code length to determine the extent of the project.
3.  **Reuse-Based Metrics:** Seven metrics assessed the amount of reusable material incorporated into the project.
4.  **Size-Metrics Complexities:** Six metrics examined aspects such as connectivity, linkage complexity, and visual complexities to gauge the intricacy of the project.
5.  **Confounding Factor Metrics:** Five metrics investigated issues influencing the outcome of the project, such as developer skillset and choice of programming language.
6.  **Analyzing Effectiveness of the Model:** Measures of error were computed and compared to the observed data to validate the efficacy of the prediction models.
7.  **Findings and Recommendations:** Certain patterns emerged indicating particular combinations of metrics contributed significantly to variations in completion time.

**Analysis Summary**:

The study identifies and examines influential metrics impacting the effort invested in web design and authoring. Through empirical investigation and comprehensive analysis, they establish correlations between specific factors and offer insights useful for constructing reliable prediction models for these endeavors. Despite limitations acknowledged in their methodology and results, the research demonstrates promising avenues toward facilitating enhanced estimation capabilities, thereby aiding decision-making in numerous settings requiring strategic choices about investments in web development initiatives.

**Used Metrics as Predictors**:

The following metrics served as predictors for estimating web design and authoring effort in the research:

1.  Product-length metrics (such as page count)
2.  Complexity-metrics (including connectivity, cyclomatic complexity)
3.  Reusability metrics (total reused media, reused program count)
4.  Reuse-based metrics (reuse frequency, reused media percentage)
5.  Structural metrics (connectivity density, structure measurement)

These chosen metrics reveal the importance of factoring diverse considerations beyond mere line counts or straightforward quantity assessments when gauging the magnitude of time needed for completing such projects.
