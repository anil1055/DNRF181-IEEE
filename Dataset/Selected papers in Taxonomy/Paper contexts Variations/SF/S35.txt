**Abstract**

*In the field of Software Engineering, several empirical*
*methods have been developed to model software projects*
*before they are undertaken to provide an estimate of re-­*
*quired* *effort,* *development time and* *cost.* *In the* *case of*
*web applications, this process is* *complicated* *by their com-­*
*plexity, multi-tiered nature, extensive use of non-code ar-­*
*tifacts such as multimedia and often short time-scales. In*
*this paper we describe a simple, highly adaptable model*
*using COSMIC Full Function Points for application size*
*measurement and design patterns as a measurement ref-­*
*erence. Rather than a true derived model the aim is to pro-­*
*vide a procedural framework for expert judgement which*
*guides the practitioner through the* *estimation* *process,*
*seeking* *to limit or mitigate variance in their judgement*
*through algorithmic or statistical techniques. This hybrid*
*has so far proven as accurate as expert judgement while*
*remaining capable of application by a relatively inexperi-­*
*enced estimator.*

**1.** **Introduction**

Web Applications (a term in which we include Web
Services[l]) are generally considered among the most
difficult software constructs to size and estimate. The

prevalence of architectural layers (often integrating dis­
parate legacy systems), their atypical structure and im­
plementation from one project to another and the pre­
ponderance of non­-code artifacts (such as multimedia
objects) make the definition of descriptive metrics ex­
tremely difficult.

Mendes et **al** discuss several different measures[2, 3],
however in the early stages of a design many of the
measured artifacts are either unknown or poorly de­
fined and a design detailed enough to measure may well
be considered too time­-consuming to produce, partic­
ularly in the case of small projects. Many teams adopt
a more ad­-hoc and intuitive approach, but this has the

major drawback that the result tends to be unreliable
and difficult to repeat accurately.

The aim of this project was to develop a methodology
which could be used by small development teams with
little or no previous experience of estimation but which
was accurate enough for repeated commercial applica­
tion where more detailed methods would be considered

overkill.

In particular, we are interested in predicting the ef­
fort required to complete work defined by the Rational
Unified Process (RUP) phases when applied to small
projects or small teams. Such projects are often char­
acterised by the level of formality applied to the pro­
cesses, the complexity of the code and the timescale for
completion[4].

In developing the project, we defined four basic prin­
ciples: the use of COSMIC Full Function Points[5] as
a sizing metric, the use of design patterns as a way of
simplifying the measurement process for highly itera­
tive projects, the use of intuitive indicators of appli­
cation complexity which can be easily related to real­
world development and the use of Monte Carlo Simu­
lation to help mitigate errors of judgement.

**2.** **Sizing** **using Design Patterns and**
**COSMIC-FFP**

If we consider application structure alone for the mo­
ment, COSMIC Full Function Points[5], with it's con­
cepts of layers, boundaries, functional processes and
data movements appears the most suited to sizing
web applications as similar concepts are present in the
multi­-tiered architecture seen in most projects[6].

However, trying to break an application down into
functional processes sufficient for COSMIC measure­
ment can be a laborious task, particularly if it's design
has not been finalised.

Current development practice is that many enter­
prise applications use standard *designpatterns[7]* which
exhibit relatively consistent functional characteristics

**2.1.** **Application Design**

Given a set of functional user requirements, we
can identify interactions between end­-users (includ­
ing other systems) across the application boundary.
These can be represented in a variety of ways ac­
cording to personal preference or local practice. The
Web Application Extension for UML[9, 10], for ex­
ample, can be used to chart interactions from the
user perspective (UX Model) which can later be ex­
panded into Design and Implementation models show­
ing architectural features.

From the UX model we can identify where our chosen
design patterns will be used and document the model,
view, control, filter & data access interactions that
make up our application along with any data groups
moved in each interaction. In doing so, we gain the


benefit of viewing our application as sets of coarsely de­
fined, interacting functional processes making it ideal
for the application of COSMIC measurement.

**3.** **Application Mapping**

**3.1.** **Viewpoint**

For the purposes of this study, we size applications
only from the *Developer* *Viewpoint* [5, pp29]. Sizing
from the *End-User* *Viewpoint* gives us an indication of
end­-user delivered functionality, but does not allow us
to take into account the complexities involved in con­
structing a multi­-tiered application which interacts ex­
tensively with other systems.

**3.2.** **Identifying Layers**

Rollo[l 1] indicates that four layers may be consid­
ered when applying COSMIC­-FFP to web applications:
application, middleware, operating system & device
drivers. In his example, the application layer contains
the client browser while the middleware layer contains
the application server, database and message broker
systems. So, if we exclude any extensive use of client­
side functionality and the presence of the operating sys­
tem, the entire web application architecture falls into
the middleware layer.

This may seem like a fairly coarse layer structure, but
it is dictated by one important principle of layer iden­
tification **which** states: *"Software that shares* *data with*
*other software shall not be considered to be in* *different*
*layers if they identically interpret the data attributes they*
*share."* [5, pp31]. By this definition, our web applica­
tion components are considered to be peer components
because they interpret the data attributes at the same
level of abstraction.

**3.3.** **Identifying Boundaries**

The COSMIC­-FFP Manual[5, pp33] states that "...
*there* *is a* *boundary between* *any* *two distinct pieces* *of*
*software in the same layer if they exchange data in* *peer-­*
*to-pcer communications".* It follows, therefore, that
HTML Documents, CGI Scripts, JSP's, servlets, En­
terprise Java Beans, Java Beans, etc, are all software
components **within** this definition.

However, if we treat each such component as a func­
tional process with method calls & returns counted
as entry/exit pairs then we are in danger of break­
ing the COSMIC measurement rules of functional size
aggregation[5, pp51] by treating every "building block"
of our application as a separate functional process.

Rather we must maintain a certain level of abstrac­

ponents implementing those processes.

**3.4.** **Identifying Functional Processes**

If we accept the layer & boundary definitions given
above, then the functional processes that constitute our
web application fall into a single layer and are consid­
ered peer processes. However, it is useful to distinguish
between the *types* of functional processes we will mea­
sure both for the purpose of identification and for later
classification **in** our productivity model. Consequently,
three types of process ­- based on the MVC model ­- can
be identified:

  - **Control** Processes are processes which are trig­

gered by events from "users" outside the appli­
cation boundary or other software components
within the application boundary, and are respon­
sible for directing application flow. For measure­
ment purposes, we consider a triggering event to
initiate a different control [functional] process if
the trigger involves the movement of a unique
data group across the component boundary, ex­
cept where a data group is a sub­-set of another
data group [5, pp36]. Intercepting Filters are con­
sidered to be control processes because they too
are triggered by an end­-user interaction with the
application.

  - **View** Processes are processes which return data

to "users" across the application boundary, carry­
ing out any aggregation and formatting required.
A single View Component may encapsulate sev­
**eral** different view processes, the presentation of
which depends on the data group that accompa­
nies it's triggering event. Where this is the case, we
would consider each different view to be associated

**with** a unique data group and be a distinct func­
tional process. Variations on a single view, such as
re­-ordering of items in a list based on a data at­
tribute, are considered to be a single functional

process.

  - **Model Processes** implement business, data ac­
cess or messaging client logic. These may include


simple Java classes, Java Beans or Enterprise Java
Beans, JDBC or JMS clients. Each business logic
or data access component may encapsulate sev­
eral distinct functional processes, each defined by
the movement of a unique data group across its
boundary.

cesses and how they interact within a web application
are shown **in** figures 2 and 3.

**3.5.** **Identifying Data Attributes and Data**
**Groups**

Identifying data groups which pass across the bound­
ary between HTTP Client and Server may be open to
some subjective analysis and debate. An initial stance
might be that the HTTP Request itself constitutes a
single data group, even though it may contain several

data attributes such as the target URL, HTTP head­
ers and form or URL­-encoded data. For example, a
single HTTP Request may contain form data repre­
senting a customer's name, address, order details and
credit card details. Considering this to be a single data
group would oversimplify the data movement and re­
sult in a false measurement. Similarly, the HTTP Re­
sponse could be thought of as being a single data group
­- the HTML code ­- even though it may present several
different data attributes.

Taking the principle given in [5, pp37] that *"Each data*
*group must be directly related to one object of interest de-­*
*scribed in the Functional User Requirements ... ",* we can
see that the above example should more properly result
in a count of 3 data groups (customer details, order de­
tails, credit card details). Since they are comprised of
data attributes which *"describe or represent"* the user's
world, they constitute valid data groups as defined un­
der the definition of data attributes described in [5,
pp41].

Whether we classify the HTTP request itself as an
additional data group, even if it carries no FUR

referenced data attributes is another matter. Under the

definitions presented in [5, pp41] for the identification
of data attributes we could classify the attributes of the
HTTP Request as either *contextual* or *implementation*
*technical.* If we consider the attributes of the HTTP Re­-­
quest to be implementation technical data then they
are invalid for the purposes of identifying data groups,
and should not be counted. This would mean, how­
ever, that a static web page could not have an entry
data movement to trigger it's functional process, since
if we consider the HTTP Request alone to have no valid
data attributes then there is no data group to consti­
tute a data movement and the triggering event becomes
invalid.

We should, therefore, consider the HTTP Request to
be contextual data as it can be interpreted under the
definition of such to be a *"type of data which specifies*
*what, when or how other data are to be processed by the*
*measured software"* [5, pp41]. This means that even a
simple web page would involve the entry data move­
ment of a single data group. The final count for the
above example would, therefore, be 4 data groups (3
descriptive and one contextual) .

Some consideration should be given to what consti­
tutes a data group in the HTTP Response as well.
Here, if we do not consider the HTML code to be a
data group in it's own right (as it is simply a presenta­
tion mechanism for the client) we have the same prob­
lem as we did with the HTTP Request. A static web
page responds with no data other than the HTML code
itself which, by this definition, is actually an invalid
data movement. We should therefore consider the ad­
ditional data contained within the HTTP Response ­HTML code, response headers & cookies ­- to be con­
textual data attributes which constitute a data group
themselves. This means that even an empty static web
page responds with at least one data movement which
we consider to be an Exit.

An HTTP Response is also likely to contain several
data groups by the definitions given in [5]. Using the
above example again, if we sent a response back to our
end­-user displaying their customer details, order details
and payment method, we would again use the princi­
ple of associating data groups with "objects of interest
from the FUR" and count 4 data groups in this data
movement (3 descriptive and 1 contextual).

to persistent storage from the application's view­

**4.2.** **Applying the Measurement Function**

With the functional processes and their data move­
ments identified we can now produce a functional size
measurement using the principles outlined in [5, pp50j.
We count 1 CFSU for each data movement ­- each in­
stance of an entry, exit, read or write of a data group
across a process boundary.

**7.** **Effort Probability Function**

Using a single value for each of our cost drivers in
the effort equation (3) gives us a point­-value estimate
for the manpower requirement to complete the project
and, while this is arguably the "most likely" figure
based on the expert judgement or historic data which
set the size and productivity values it may not be
the statistically most­-likely figure. Factors with wide
ranges are likely to have a significant effect on our fi­
nal manpower figure and so we should attempt to cal­
culate the probability of these extreme results for the
purposes of determining a more statistically likely re­
sult and associated risk identification.

We can obtain a statistically most­-likely figure, and
generate additional data we can use for risk analysis by
using *Monte Carlo Simulation* to generate a probabil­
ity function for our manpower figure. Monte Carlo Sim­
ulation works by randomly choosing sets of cost driver
values from the available ranges and calculating ap­
plication size, productivity and related effort based on
those values. If this operation is carried out a signifi­
cant number of times (say, 1000+) we can use the re­
sulting data to generate a probability distribution for
effort indicating how likely a given result is for the in­
put data.

**7.1.** **Monte-Carlo Simulation**

In determining our factors, we can use expert judge­
ment, group consensus or analysis of historic data to
specify three values for each factor: the lowest expected
value, the highest expected value and the most likely
expected value. These three values can be used to spec­
ify a probability function *(J(x))* for each factor.

In our case study, the triangular distribution was used
for simplicity, but ultimately any probability distribu­
tion based on the three specified values can be used.

We can then determine a given value of the factor, X,
by generating a random number from a uniform distri­
bution U[O, 1] and applying it to the inverse of *f(x).*

### 8. Scheduling and Resource Planning

Knowing the effort required to complete a project
gives us some idea of the scale of the project but in
itself gives us little idea of the calendar time or man­
power required.

The COPSEMO model[l4] takes a COCOMO II ef­
fort estimate in person­-months and predicts the calen­
dar time required to complete the project spread across
the four MBASE/RUP phases (Inception, Elaboration,
Construction & Transition). While this method re­
mains valid, it assumes that neither schedule or man­
power are constrained in any way, predicting a sched­
ule based on principles of best use of manpower across
calendar time derived from historic project data.

However, in reality, many projects are constrained ei­
ther by schedule or available manpower. Where either
is specified, we should determine what the other, un­
constrained value is and where both are specified we

should determine whether the constraints represent a
feasible project.

We can do this by starting with either a maximum fi­
nal schedule (in days) or a maximum number of avail­
able personnel and working backwards from these fig­
ures using principles defined in the COPSEMO model.

In the *case* of constrained manpower, we assume that
the Construction phase uses the specified maximum
number of personnel, then derive the manpower re­
quirements and schedule for the other phases relative
to that. In the *case* of a constrained schedule we assume

that the specified time is 125% of the total schedule and
derive the values for the four phases from there. Fur­
ther calculation then gives us the manpower require­
ments for the phases.

If we keep our initial estimates of size and productiv­
ity constant (assuming that incorrect setting of some of
the model factors was responsible for the error in our
estimate) we can apply a correction to each of our size
and productivity factors such that the ratio of size fac­
tors to productivity factors is the same as the ratio
of actual effort to predicted effort. We can then deter­
mine, for our stated project conditions, what each of
the applicable factors *should* have been.

For example, if our predicted effort was 44 man­-days
but the project took only 41 man­-days. Our correc­
tion factor is 0.934. We have decided that the layout
complexity (LAYC) and execution constraint (EXEC)
size factors were not as high as they should have been
so we increase both by 10%. We must then adjust
our productivity factors so that the correct ratio of
size to productivity factor corrections (0.934) is ob­
tained. Here, we have decided that the requirements
were more volatile than expected (REQV) and so de­
creased our productivity, some of the RAD practices
we used helped (RADP), some changes in platform
(PLAV) that were expected failed to materialise, ven­
dor support was very good (SUPT) and our program­
mers performed better than expected (PCAP, DOMF).

We can now multiply the minimum, most likely and
maximum values of our original size & productivity fac­
tors by these corrective values to give a new set of fac­
tors. Using these factors, we can recalculate our ad­
justed size, productivity and hence predicted effort ­which now agrees with the actual effort from the com­
pleted project.

**9.3.** **Calibration Using Historic Data**

While initial, and indeed ongoing, calibration of the
model can be carried out by using expert judgement as
defined above, it is possible to refine that expert judge­
ment over time by reference to completed projects and
historic data.

Once a project is completed we have a record of the

[corrected] size & productivity factor values and de­
scriptions of the conditions which determined those val­
ues. With this data recorded, it can be used to deter­
mine potential factor values for other projects through
analogy or statistical aggregation. In other words, if
the particular conditions for one or more factors in a
new project match those of a completed project then
we can use the [corrected] factors from the completed
project as a guideline for the values in our new project.


Where we see particular conditions repeated from
project­-to­-project we can combine data available from
several projects to produce a mean value for any given
factor which we then use in new projects. Alternatively,
we can use this mean value to correct our original base­
line conditions given that once we see certain condi­
tions repeated more often than not we can assume that
they now constitute a new baseline.
### 10. Case Study

A case study was carried out from September through
November 2003 using a web application suite consist­
ing of three related applications: an end­-user applica­
tion, an end­-user administrator application and a sys­
tem administrator application. Developers were asked
to note time spent on the project, including their day­
to­-day activities (such as informal technical discussions
related to the project or coffee breaks) but excluding

Firstly, we can see that the model proved to be quite
accurate for the project as a whole. It predicted an ef­
fort of 103.84 man­-days against an actual effort of 100
man­-days, an error of 3.84 man­-days and a resulting

a development time of 66.88 working days against an
actual time of 50 working days, so we over­-estimated by
16.88 days (or about 3 weeks). In this case, the sched­
ule over­-estimation was considered to be fortuitous as
customer­-release had been announced for January 2004
(based on this estimate) and the company was able to
deliver almost a month early ­- a fact much appreci­
ated by the customers.

When we look at the results for individual projects, we
can see a much greater margin of error. As all three ap­
plications had a common database much of it's design
was completed as part of the first application, the End­
User application. As such, the predicted effort of 22.42
man­-days was overrun by a little over a week (30 man­
days total), however our schedule required only one de­
veloper for Inception & Elaboration while in reality two
developers were assigned full­-time to the project so the
additional effort was recovered and the final schedule

was only half a day from our estimate. For this first ap­
plication, our MRE was therefore 33.81%.

The other two applications resulted in slightly bet­
ter MREs of 15.25% and 12.2%, both of which repre­
sented overestimates. Again, this was due in part to
the fact that two developers had been assigned full­
time and partly due to the fact that development was
found to be easier than expected.

While the applications were sized and estimated sep­
arately, it is obvious that there was significant over­
lap between the applications in terms of development
work, particularly during the inception and elabora­
tion phases. Extra time spent here developing a con­
sistent design (particularly for the database) for the
three applications resulted in significant savings dur­
ing construction. When the results for the three appli­
cations are combined and the effect these overlapping
elements eliminated, the results were extremely encour­
aging. A prediction error of only 4 man­-days across a
three month project is believed to be much more ac­
curate than expert judgement had been on previous
projects.


**11.** **Conclusions and Future Work**

The aim of the project was to produce a repeatable
and accurate framework which could be adopted by rel­
atively inexperienced estimators, including the average
developer. As such, the emphasis was always on sim­
plicity and practicality. Despite this relatively simple
nature, the model has proven (in it's single tested case
study so far) to be surprisingly accurate. Given the fact
that it had never been applied in a real test case before
and was used by a development team who were techni­
cally capable but unfamiliar with it, this result is en­
couraging.

The team readily took to several aspects of the model.
The fact that many of the terms used were well known
to them made it easy to adopt and discuss applica­
tion of the model in design meetings. In many cases
discussion of the various application size and produc �
tivity factors helped the team to anticipate and high­
light potential problem areas in the development pro­

cess.

The general opinion was that the model provided a
reasonably accurate estimate in terms of effort but
did tend to over­-estimate the project schedule ­- which
was not necessarily seen as a bad thing. The use of
COSMIC­-FFP proved to be the most difficult part of
the process, but once the basic concepts and proce­
dure were explained in detail it was seen as being rel­
atively straightforward to apply ­- although interpreta­
tion of the measurement principles was often a point
of debate.

Undoubtedly, the model needs to be applied to sev­
eral more case studies to confirm it's accuracy against a
wider range of projects and architectures. Specifically,
it's application to different design patterns and archi­
tectures other than J2EE (Microsoft .NET, for exam­
ple) would go some way towards proving it's efficacy in
a variety of cases.

Further tool development to remove the reliance on
several disparate spreadsheets and simulator code is
needed, specifically to bring the process under the con­
trol of a single application which can be used to deter­
mine, maintain & record project data in a single data­
store and further automate the estimation process.
