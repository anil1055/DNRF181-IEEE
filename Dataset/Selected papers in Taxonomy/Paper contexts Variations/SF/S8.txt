**ABSTRACT**
Accurate estimates of development effort play an important
role in the successful management of larger hypermedia
development projects. By applying measurement principles
to measure characteristics of the applications and their
development processes, feedback can be obtained to help
understand, control and improve future applications and
corresponding processes.

The objective of this paper is to describe the application of
case-based reasoning to estimating the effort for developing
hypermedia applications. The data used in the estimation
process was obtained through an experiment where effort
and size metrics were collected.

We have shown that case-based reasoning is a candidate
technique to effort estimation and, with the aid of an
automated environment, can be applied to hypermedia
development effort prediction.

**KEYWORDS:** effort prediction, hypermedia development,
case-based reasoning.

**INTRODUCTION**
In any scientific field, measurement generates quantitative
descriptions of processes and products, making it possible
to understand behaviour and result. This understanding
allows for the selection of better techniques and tools to
control and improve processes, products and resources [19].

Software practitioners recognise the importance of realistic
estimates of effort for the successful management of large
software projects, hypermedia applications are no
exception.

Using realistic estimates at an early stage in a project's life
cycle allow project managers and development
organisations to manage resources effectively.

Prediction, a necessary part of an effective process, be it
authoring, design, testing, or development, comprises [5]:
step 1. Capturing data from past projects (software and
development processes) which can be used to
estimate effort.
step 2. Generation of prediction models based on historical
data, collected during step 1.
step 3. Evaluation of how effective those models are.

This paper focuses on effort prediction for design and
authoring processes. We adopt the classification proposed
by Lowe and Hall [13] where authoring encompasses the
management of activities for the actual content and
structure of the application and its presentation. Design
covers the methods used for generating the structure and
functionality of the application, and typically does not
include aspects such as application requirements elicitation,
feasibility consideration and applications maintenance. In
addition, our design phase also incorporates the conceptual
design of the application, reflected in map diagrams
showing documents and links.

As effort is the largest and least predictable component of
project costs, most approaches found in the literature focus
on this measure [5]. In this paper we only address design
and authoring effort.

The data used in the estimation process was gathered
through an experiment [6] where a set of suggested metrics
for effort prediction were measured. The metrics were
classified as size, effort and confounding factors [12].

A number of size metrics from the literature in Software
Engineering and Multimedia were reused [5,9,25] or
adapted [15]. The effort for authoring and designing
hypermedia applications was estimated using Case-based
reasoning (CBR) [11,21] also called estimation by analogy.


The rationale for CBR is to characterise the project, for
which the estimate is to be made, relative to a number of
attributes (e.g. application complexity, link complexity etc).
This description is then used to find other similar projects,
already finished, and an estimate for the new project is
made based on the known effort values for those finished
projects.

This paper is organised as follows: Section 2 presents
related work in development effort prediction for
hypermedia applications. Section 3 presents in more depth
the technique of case-based reasoning and in Section 4 we
describe the tool used to automate the estimation process.
In Section 5 we present the experiment and Section 6
describes the empirical results of applying CBR. Finally,
Section 7 concludes with an analysis of the strengths and
weaknesses of using CBR and suggestions on future work.

**CASE-BASED REASONING**
Case-based Reasoning involves [21]:
� Characterising the project for which an estimate is
required, i.e., identifying project attributes (application
size, link complexity etc) which can influence effort.
� Use of this characterisation as a basis for finding
similar (analogous) completed projects, for which
effort is known.
� Use these effort values, possibly with adjustment, to
generate a predicted value of effort.

Finding the right analogies and assessing the degree of
similarity are the difficulties of this method.

When using CBR there are a number of issues to consider

[23]:
� How to best characterise a project? The range of
attributes must be limited to the information available
at the point that the estimation is required. Attributes
should portray the project as precisely as possible and
it is essential to choose at least one attribute that
characterises project in terms of size.
� How to detect similarities (analogies) between projects
and with what level of confidence?
� How to identify the number of analogies to search for?
� How an effort estimate for a new project can be
derived from analogous finished projects? Options are
to use the mean effort of all analogous finished
projects; to calculate a weighted mean of efforts where
more influence is placed on the most similar finished
projects (closer analogies).

Once there is a dataset containing historical data of
finished projects, effort for new projects can then be
estimated.

The analogies used in our work were calculated according
to the following steps:
Step 1) Find the best combination of attributes to give the
best estimate.
Step 2) Use the results obtained in step 1 to find the most
similar finished project(s) to the new project, for
which effort needs to be estimated. During this
step it is also necessary to decide the number of
projects (analogies) to be used in the estimation,


which can vary from 1 to total-number-ofcompleted-projects-available [2] .
Step 3) Calculate the estimated effort as the mean of the
known effort values for those projects selected in
step 2 [3] .

A detailed description of CBR and a survey of several
effort prediction models is given in [10,21].

**THE ANGEL-PLUS SOFTWARE TOOL**
It is feasible to find analogies, using the method described
in Section 3, without the help of a computer program,
however it can be both time consuming and error prone as
the number of projects or attributes increase. Consequently,
we have used a case-based reasoning effort estimation tool
which automates the process and provides an environment
where data can be stored, analogies found and estimates
generated. The tool was developed at Bournemouth
University under the name of ANaloGy softwarE tooL Plus
(ANGEL-Plus). An important feature of ANGEL-Plus is
the ability to determine the optimum combination of
attributes for finding analogies.

Figure 1 (see appendix A) shows part of a template for
recording project data. Templates are used to describe the
data used in the estimation process and can be configured
to match the specific data collection environment of an
organisation. As ANGEL-Plus does not impose any
particular set of attributes the estimation process can take
optimal advantage of the data available. The user chooses
the number of attributes and their names, the only
exceptions are the project number, status and actual effort
which are compulsory. ANGEL-Plus uses the project
number to uniquely identify each project. Status shows
whether or not a project is “on” or “off”, representing
respectively a completed project which can be used as a
source of analogy or a new/ongoing project. The actual
effort is mandatory for all completed projects as it supplies
the foundation for prediction. Figure 2 (Appendix A)
shows the data collected for the dataset used in our work.

Once the template has been created and data entered, the
next step is to find the optimum combination of attributes
used to find analogies. The justification behind the
selection of attributes is that not necessarily all attributes
collected will be useful to finding good analogies. The user
can select all or a subset of attributes described in the
template.

**THE EXPERIMENT**
The experiment [6] consisted of the design and authoring of
hypermedia applications aimed at teaching a chosen topic,
structured according to the Cognitive Flexibility Theory
(CFT) principles [24], using a minimum of 50 documents.
The delivery system (environment) chosen was the World
Wide Web, as this was the one most students were familiar
with.

Forty three Computer Science students from the University
of Auckland participated in the experiment. They were
attending the Hypermedia and Multimedia Systems course,
offered during the first semester of 2000. Two
questionnaires were used to collect the data. The first [4]

asked subjects to rate their Web authoring experience using
five scales, from no experience (zero) to very good
experience (four). The second questionnaire [5] was used to
measure characteristics of the Web applications developed
(suggested metrics) and the effort involved in designing
and authoring those applications. On both questionnaires,
we describe in depth each scale type, to avoid any
misunderstanding. Members of the research group checked
both questionnaires for ambiguous questions, unusual tasks,
number of questions and definitions in the Appendix.

Early analysis showed that six questionnaires were missing
fundamental information. They were not used in the data
analysis, leaving thirty seven datapoints.

To reduce learning effects, subjects were given a
coursework prior to designing and authoring the Web
applications, which consisted of:
� Creating a simple personal homepage.
� Designing a multi-page Web application.

�
Creating a Web site for the Matakohe Kauri Museum [6],
improving on their existing site.
� Loading the Web pages onto a Web server.

**Validity of the Experiment**
Here we present our comments on the validity of the
experiment:

- The metrics collected, apart from effort, experience
and structure, were all objective and precisely
quantifiable. The scales used to measure *experience*
and *structure* were described in detail in both
questionnaires.

- Subjects' authoring and design experiences were
mostly scaled little or average, with a low difference
between skill levels. Consequently the original dataset
was left intact.

- To reduce maturation effects, i.e. learning effects
caused by subjects learning as an experiment proceeds,
subjects had to develop each a Web application as part
of their first assignment. They also received training in
the CFT principles.

- All applications used a hierarchical structure.

- Notepad (or similar text editor) and FirstPage were the
two tools most frequently used. Notepad is a simple
text editor while FirstPage is freeware offering buttonembedded HTML tags. Although they differ with
respect to the functionality offered, data analysis
revealed that the corresponding effort was similar,
suggesting that confounding effects from the tools
were controlled.

- As the subjects who participated in the experiment
were either Computer Science Honours or MSc
students, it is likely that they present skill sets similar
to Web professionals at the start of their careers. The
use of students as subjects, while sometimes
considered unrealistic, is justified for two reasons:
firstly, empirical evidence by Bohem-Davis and Ross

[1] indicates that students are equal to professionals in
many quantifiable measures, including their approach
to developing software; secondly, for pragmatic
considerations, having students as subjects was the
only viable option for this experiment.

**EMPIRICAL RESULTS USING CBR**
In order to evaluate how precise the estimations were using
CBR, we followed seven steps:
Step 1 One datapoint was chosen and removed from the
dataset. This datapoint was considered as a new
project for the sake of this evaluation.
Step 2 The remaining 36 datapoints were kept in the
dataset and used in the estimation process.
Step 3 For the removed project, its actual effort data was
discarded, and only the known size metrics kept.
This was done in an attempt to simulate a new
project for use with ANGEL-Plus to calculate
estimated effort.
Step 4 The optimum combination of attributes was
generated by using the "Best attribute set" option.


Step 5 Effort was estimated using 1 to 3 analogies,
looking for the estimated effort closest to the
removed project’s discarded actual effort.
Step 6 The datapoint which had been removed on Step 1
was added back to the dataset.
Step 7 Steps 2 through 6 were repeated until all 37
projects had been removed and had had their effort
estimated.

The results obtained (see Table 2) are as follows:

- Except for one datapoint, the best combination of
attributes for the whole dataset was *Media Count*,
*Total Node Allocation* and *Total Reused Media*
*Allocation*, suggesting that the number of media files
used by an application and the space allocated for
nodes and reused media may be good indicators of


effort. Further analysis of our dataset showed that there
was a slight negative relationship between effort and
both *Total Reused Media Allocation* and *Reused Media*
*Count,* suggesting that the higher the amount of reuse
the smaller the effort. The relationship between reuse
and effort/productivity has received considerable
attention in the literature [5,7,13,17,22] as it seems that
level of reuse is inversely related to effort.

- The average percentage of error between actual effort
and estimated effort was of 9.21%, indicating a very
good prediction power [21].

- One analogy, followed by three analogies, presented
the most accurate estimates.

How can the results and prediction methodology presented
in this paper be of benefit to hypermedia/Web developers?
In whatever type of hypermedia application (educational,
fiction etc) there is at least one phase of their development
which is common to all - authoring. Furthermore, most
hypermedia systems reported in the literature provide
environments to author static hypermedia applications

[3,4,14]. Consequently, the metrics suggested in this paper
may be of interest and useful to a wider range of
hypermedia authors other than Web authors. In addition,
the prediction methodology we have used can be applied to
any organisation and to any type of hypermedia application
and development environment.

In summary, there is an increased need for effective
management of time and resources in organisations
undertaking large numbers of projects and measurement
principles can help understand, control and improve
processes and products.

**CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK**
This paper presented the application of Case-based
reasoning to estimating Web authoring and design effort.
The dataset used was obtained through an experiment in
which we measured Application, Process and Resource
metrics.

The analogy was performed using an estimation tool ANGEL-Plus. The average percentage of error between
actual effort and estimated effort was of 9.21%, which
represents very good predictions. This result suggests that
Case-based reasoning is a candidate technique to effort
estimation and that with the aid of an automated
environment it is a practical technique to apply to
hypermedia development effort prediction.

As part of our future work we plan to apply effort
prediction to the whole development cycle of hypermedia
applications and to compare the performance of case-based
reasoning against human estimation and regression
techniques, in a similar way to [8,16,18].

To conclude, there is an urgent need for adequate
hypermedia development effort prediction at an early stage
in the development. As the use of hypermedia, and in
particular the Web, as a delivery environment increases,
effort estimation can contribute significantly to the

reduction of costs and time involved in developing
hypermedia applications.
