**Summary of "Circumstantial-Evidence-Based Effort Judgement for Web Service Composition-Based SOA Implementations" Paper**

The paper discusses the challenge of effort estimation for Web Service Composition (WSC)-based Service Oriented Architecture (SOA) implementations. The authors propose a circumstantial-evidence-based effort judgement approach to overcome the difficulties associated with quantitatively investigating the effort of various WSC approaches.

**Key Points:**

1.  The authors suggest treating a WSC-based SOA system as a mechanically organised entity, borrowing concepts from organisation theory to guide their research in the area of service-oriented software engineering.
2.  They use the Divide-and-Conquer (D&C) strategy, a classic problem-solving approach borrowed from organisation theory, to break down the effort judgement for a whole WSC-based SOA implementation into separate tasks for individual WSCs.
3.  The authors create an effort-oriented classification matrix to categorize WSC approaches along two dimensions: context and process. The context dimension considers pattern, semiotics, mechanism, design-time, and run-time considerations, while the process dimension looks at workflow-based, model-driven, AI-planning technologies.
4.  Due to the lack of readily available data on effort estimation for various WSC approaches, the researchers opt for a circumstantial-evidence-based judgement approach instead. Circumstantial evidence refers to indirect proof derived from observations, events, or circumstances suggesting something might happen, whereas direct evidence establishes a direct link between a piece of evidence and what you're trying to prove.
5.  They develop a framework to systematically collect circumstantial evidence, leveraging Systematic Literature Reviews (SLRs) to extract effort-related hypotheses. 
6.  Circumstantial evidence is utilised via the Theory of Propositional Learning (TPL) for diagnostic reasoning. This enables the creation of a logical chain connecting potential explanations and observed phenomena.
7.  The authors present a case study involving a railway supply company called RailCo Ltd., demonstrating the effectiveness of their circumstantial-evidence-based approach in comparing different WSC approaches' effort scores for a given WSC project.
8.  Key findings indicate that their approach provides a useful basis for evaluating the relative effort of different WSC approaches, facilitating the determination of the most efficient course of action.

**Predictors Used as Effort Estimators:**

1.  Contextual Factors:

    *   Pattern (orchestration and choreography)
    *   Semiotics (semantics and syntax)
    *   Mechanisms (SOAP and REST)
    *   Design-Time Processes (manual, semi-automatic, and automatic)
    *   Runtime Environment (static and dynamic)
2.  Process Dimensions:

    *   Workflow-Based Technologies
    *   Model-Driven Approaches
    *   Artificial Intelligence Planning Techniques
3.  Specific Features Considered in Each Category:
    *   Complexity of Methods Used
        *   Internal Environmental Complexities
            o   Difficulty Levels Behind Process Models
                \- Human Activities Involved
                    \- Tools Adopted
                        \- Technically Sophisticatedness
                            \- Level of Tool Support
