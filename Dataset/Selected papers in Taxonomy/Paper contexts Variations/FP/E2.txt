# **Web Metrics and Development Effort Prediction**

Emilia Mendes and Nile Mosley

**ABSTRACT**
The World Wide Web (Web) has become the best known example of a hypermedia system. To
date, numerous organisations world-wide have developed thousands of commercial and/or
educational Web applications. However, developing good quality applications has a high cost,
mostly in time and amount of difficulty involved for the authors. By applying measurement
principles to measure the quality of applications and their development processes, feedback can
be obtained to help understand, control and improve products and processes. In addition, effort
prediction models for Web development can be developed using the predictor metrics obtained.

The objective of this paper is to present a Web development prediction model based on a set of
metrics collected using a case study evaluation. The case study used fourth year Computer
Science students as subjects to develop Web applications using both static and dynamic pages.
All applications were constrained to a structure outlined in an instructional theory, the Cognitive
Flexibility Theory.

To define the prediction model we applied DeMarcos's five steps in defining local cost models,
given as:
� Decompose cost elements.
� Formulate cost theory.
� Collect data.

� Analyse data and evaluate model.
� Check model.

The first part of this paper describes the case study evaluation (CSE) by which metrics used in
the prediction model were collected. The second half presents the proposed prediction model,
generated using stepwise regression analysis, and assesses its prediction power using
the Mean Magnitude of Relative Error and the Prediction at level 25 (Pred(25)).

**1** **INTRODUCTION**
The World Wide Web, or simply The Web, has become the best known example of a
hypermedia system. To date, numerous organisations world-wide have developed many
thousands of commercial and/or educational Web applications. However, developing good
quality applications has a high cost, mostly in time and degree of difficulty involved for the
authors [1]. Feedback from applying measurement principles to measure the quality of
applications and their development processes can help understand, control and improve products
and processes. Effort prediction models for Web development can also be proposed, based on
the predictor metrics obtained.

In addition, software practitioners recognise the importance of realistic estimates of effort to the
successful management of software projects, the Web being no exception. Having realistic

1

estimates at an early stage in a project's life cycle allow project managers and development
organisations to manage resources effectively.

Prediction is a necessary part of an effective process, be it authoring, design, testing, or Web
development as a whole.

To define the prediction model we applied DeMarcos's five steps in defining local cost models

[2], which are: decompose cost elements, formulate cost theory, collect data, analyse data and
evaluate model, check model.

This paper focuses on effort prediction for design and authoring processes. We adopt the
classification proposed by Lowe and Hall [3] where authoring encompasses the management of
activities for the actual content and structure of the application and its presentation. Design
covers the methods used for generating the structure and functionality of the application, and
typically, does not include aspects such as hypermedia application requirements elicitation,
feasibility consideration and applications maintenance. In addition, our design phase also
incorporates the conceptual design of the application, reflected in map diagrams showing
documents and links.

The data used to generate the prediction model was gathered using a quantitative case study
evaluation in which a set of suggested size metrics and cost drivers for effort prediction were
measured [2,4,5,6]. The Prediction model proposed was generated by a statistical technique,
specifically Stepwise Multiple Regression.

Section 2 describes the case study evaluation and Section 3 presents the prediction model and
corresponding prediction power. Finally, we give our conclusions and comments on future work
in Section 4.

**2** **THE CASE STUDY EVALUATION**

**2.1 Introduction**

The case study evaluation measured several attributes (metrics) of Web applications and the
effort involved in Web design and authoring. The attributes reflect our notion of product size,
complexity, characteristics of the application, process and resources that are expected to
influence effort in some way. A Product represents a Web application.

The case study consisted of the design and authoring of Web applications to teach a given topic
chosen by the subjects. Each application was structured according to the Cognitive Flexibility
Theory (CFT) principles [7] using a minimum of 50 Web pages.

Forty-three Computer Science students from the University of Auckland participated in the case
study, attending the Hypermedia and Multimedia Systems course during the first semester of
2000. Two questionnaires were used to collect the data. The first [1] asked subjects to rate their
Web authoring experience using five scales, from no experience (one) to very good experience
(five). The second questionnaire [2] was used to measure characteristics of the Web applications
developed (suggested metrics) and the effort involved in designing and authoring those
applications. For both questionnaires, we described in depth each scale type, to avoid any

**2.3 Validity of the Case Study**
There were four confounding factors in the case study evaluation:
i) A subject's authoring and designing experience.
ii) Maturation effects, i.e. learning effects caused by subjects learning as an experiment
proceeds.
iii) Structure of the application.
iv) Tools used to help author and design the Web application.

The data collected revealed:

i) Subjects' authoring and design experiences were mostly scaled little (experience=2) or
average (experience=3), with a low skill differential [7] . A scatterplot between experience and
effort helped investigate their relationship. Most datapoints fell within clusters in the
interval between 10 to 80 hours, either for experience 2 or 3. Consequently the original
dataset was left whole.

ii) Subjects had to develop a Web application as part of their first assignment. They also
received training in the CFT principles, reducing maturation effects.
iii) Hierarchy was the structure used by nearly all the subjects.
iv) Notepad (or similar text editor) and FirstPage were the two tools used the most. Notepad is a
very simple text editor. FirstPage is freeware offering button-embedded HTML tags,
removing the need to remember the tags. Although they differ with respect to the
functionality offered, a scatterplot between total-effort and tool revealed that most
datapoints fell within the interval between 10 to 80 hours, either using Notepad or FirstPage.
Consequently, confounding effects from the tools were reduced.

**3** **PREDICTION MODEL AND POWER**

**3.1 Introduction**

For the collected data, an algorithmic model, stepwise regression, was used to generate an effort
prediction model for Web design and authoring.

The general purpose of stepwise regression models is to learn more about the relationship
between several predictor variables and a response variable. Multiple regression allows the
researcher to ask (and hopefully answer) the general question "what is the best predictor of ...".

Regression modelling is one of the most widely used statistical modelling techniques for fitting a
quantitative response variable *y* as a function of one or more predictor variables *x* *1* *, x* *2* *,...,x* *p* .
Regression models are widely used as they often provide an excellent fit to a response variable
when the true functional relationship between the response and the predictors is unknown [8].

Stepwise regression was calculated using SPSS 10.0.5.

Relative Error (MMRE) and the Pred(25) [9].

is not limited to regression based methods [10]. The mean, used by the MMRE, takes into
account the numerical value of every single observation in the data distribution. A small MMRE
indicates a good prediction model.

It is generally accepted that algorithmic models perform better on smaller more homogenous
datasets. Although the number of Web pages for each application was similar, other variables
had greater variation and may have influenced the results obtained. For example, *Total Page*
*Allocation* varied from 129 to 728. By splitting the original datasets in an effort to make them

The results for the MMRE and Pred(25) may indicate that larger datasets may effectively be

addition, the results may also suggest that more is not necessarily better when it comes to the
sizes of the datasets used [12].

� Organisations involved in the development of Web applications/sites may build datasets

development cycle, rather than to the entire development.

**4** **CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK**
This paper has presented a case study evaluation in which we measured attributes to be used on
an effort prediction model. Some of the suggested metrics can be used in the prediction of effort
for dynamically generated Web applications. However, the metrics were mostly related to static
applications.

In addition, the paper proposes an effort prediction model generated using a statistical technique

- Stepwise Multiple Regression.

Although the prediction model generated did not in general present low MMRE and high
Pred(25), we are aware that other techniques, such as General Linear Models (GLMs), have the
advantage of allowing relationships between response and predictor variables to be non-linear,
providing considerable flexibility while still yielding to a rigorous statistical treatment. In
addition, there are also other non-algorithmic techniques which, in the same way as linear
models, allow the user to see how a model derives its conclusions. These techniques are fuzzy
systems and case-based reasoning. The use of GLMs and case-based reasoning are the scope of
our future work.

We also plan to apply effort prediction to the whole development cycle of Web applications and
to compare the performance of several prediction techniques against human estimation.

To conclude, effort estimation is an essential component of modern project management and
cost analysis. Used correctly, at a time when Web authoring skills are at a premium, well
designed Web sites, using a wide range of tools are more easily completed on time and to
budget.
